Match Scoring & Classification

Every candidate pair arrives at this stage carrying a bag of per-field similarity scores — name 0.92, email 1.0, phone 0.0, address 0.71. Your job is to collapse that bag into one verdict: match, send-to-review, or no-match. This is the heart of your ScoringPlugin, and getting the combination logic right is the difference between a clean golden record and a corrupted merge.

Where scoring sits in your pipeline
Standardize→ Block→ Self-Join→ Field Scoring→ Combine & Classify→ Cluster

By now you've built every piece that feeds this stage. Module 02 (string processing) produces the per-field similarity scores. Module 00's self-join produces the candidate pairs those scores attach to. What's been missing is the step that turns "here are six numbers about one pair" into "here is the one decision about this pair." That step is this module.

The exact problem, stated once

You have a candidate pair — two records the blocking stage thinks might be the same entity. The field scorer has already compared them column by column:

FieldRecord ARecord BSimilarity
nameJonathan SmithJon Smith0.92
emailjsmith@acme.iojsmith@acme.io1.00
phone555-0142555-99810.00
address12 Oak Ave12 Oak Avenue0.74
cityAustinAustin1.00

Five numbers. One question: is this the same person? The email is identical — that's strong. The phone is completely different — is that disqualifying, or just a second number? The name is close but not exact. How do you weigh all of that into a single answer a machine can act on? That is what the next eight stages build, piece by piece.

How to use this module: Each stage adds one mechanism — weighting, then combining, then null handling, then thresholds — and the Score Builder in Stage 07 lets you turn all the knobs at once and watch the verdict move. The Self-Test at the end is scenario-recognition, not recall: you'll be handed a pair and asked which mechanism applies.

Stage 01

The Naive Approach — and Why It Breaks

The first instinct is always the same: average the field scores. It feels reasonable, it's one line of code, and it is wrong in a way that quietly corrupts your matches. Seeing exactly how it fails is the fastest route to understanding why every real scorer is weighted.

The one-liner everyone writes first

You have five similarity scores. The obvious move is to take their mean and call that the match score:

The naive scorer
# scores for one candidate pair field_scores = { "name": 0.92, "email": 1.00, "phone": 0.00, "address": 0.74, "city": 1.00, } composite = sum(field_scores.values()) / len(field_scores) # → (0.92 + 1.00 + 0.00 + 0.74 + 1.00) / 5 # → 3.66 / 5 # → 0.732

0.732. Whether that's a "match" depends on your threshold, but hold that number — the problem isn't the number itself, it's that the average had no idea which fields actually matter. It treated a matching email and a matching city as worth exactly the same.

Two pairs the average can't tell apart

Here's the failure made concrete. Two completely different candidate pairs — and the naive average gives them the same score:

FieldPair X — simPair Y — sim
name1.000.20
email0.001.00
phone0.001.00
address1.000.40
city1.000.40
naive average0.600.60

Both average to 0.60. But look at what's actually happening:

Pair X shares a name, an address, and a city — but the emails and phones are nothing alike. That's the classic different people, same household: two roommates, a parent and child, people who once lived at the same address. Same name is coincidence-prone; same address is shared. This is probably not a match.

Pair Y has an identical email and an identical phone, with only a fuzzy name and a vaguely similar address. That's almost certainly the same person — someone who moved, married and changed their surname, or whose name was entered differently in two systems. Email + phone agreeing is hard to fake. This is very likely a match.

The corruption: the naive average scores a probable non-match and a probable match identically. If your threshold sits at 0.60, you either merge both (corrupting Pair X's household into one person) or reject both (splitting Pair Y's real entity). There is no threshold that separates them — because the information that separates them was thrown away the moment you averaged.
The fix, in one sentence: the email and phone fields should count more than name, address, and city — because agreeing on email is far stronger evidence of "same person" than agreeing on a name. That's weighting, and it's all of Stage 02.

Stage 02

Field Weighting — Not All Evidence Is Equal

A weight is a number that says how much a field's agreement should move the final verdict. Choosing weights well is the single most important judgment in entity resolution — and it rests on one idea: how good is this field at telling two different people apart?

Analogy — the courtroom
Picture two pieces of evidence in a trial. Exhibit A: the suspect's DNA was at the scene. Exhibit B: the suspect was wearing blue, and so was the perpetrator.

Both are "matches." But the DNA is nearly conclusive — almost nobody else has it. Wearing blue? Thousands of people wear blue. A good judge doesn't average the two exhibits — she weights them by how much each one narrows the field of suspects.

In your pipeline, a matching email or SSN is DNA — almost nobody shares it by accident. A matching first name or city is "they both wore blue" — common, weak, easy to share. The weight is the discriminating power of the field.

The red herring that trips everyone

This is the exact spot where it's easy to fool yourself. When you see a field with a raw similarity of 1.00, every instinct says "that's a strong match!" But the raw score and the field's importance are two different things, and confusing them is the most common scoring bug there is.

✗ Red herring — what grabs your attention
"The name matches perfectly — 1.00!"

A raw similarity of 1.00 feels decisive. The two records say "Smith" and "Smith" — exact match, top score. Surely that's strong evidence they're the same person.

The trap: you're reading the height of the score as if it were the strength of the evidence. They are not the same number.

✓ What actually decides
How many people share this value?

"Smith" is one of the most common surnames alive. Millions of unrelated people match perfectly on it. A 1.00 on name rules out almost nobody — so it deserves a small weight.

A 1.00 on email rules out almost everybody else on earth. Same raw score, vastly more discriminating — so it deserves a large weight.

Say it back to yourself: the raw similarity score is a red herring for importance. Discriminating power — how rare it is for two different people to share this value — is what sets the weight. A perfect match on a weak field is still weak.

A weight table you could ship

Here's a realistic set of weights for a person-matching pipeline, ordered by discriminating power. Weights sum to 1.0 — that's a convention that makes the composite land neatly between 0 and 1, which you'll lean on in Stage 03.

FieldDiscriminating powerWeightWhy
ssn / national idvery high0.30Near-unique by design. A match is almost conclusive on its own.
emailhigh0.25Rarely shared between two real people. Strong identity signal.
phonehigh0.20Fairly unique — but landlines and family plans get shared.
namemedium0.15Common names collide constantly; typos and nicknames are normal.
addressmedium0.07Households share it; people move. Useful, not decisive.
citylow0.03Thousands share a city. Almost no power to distinguish alone.
total1.00
These numbers are a starting point, not law. Real weights get tuned against labeled data — you measure which fields actually separate true matches from false ones in your data and adjust. That tuning loop is Module 05. For now, the relative ordering (id > email > phone > name > address > city) is the durable part; the exact decimals are negotiable.

Where weights live in your pipeline

In a YAML-driven plugin like yours, weights are config, not code. They sit in the plugin's parameters so an analyst can retune them without touching Python or redeploying:

scoring_plugin.yaml — weights as config
- plugin: ScoringPlugin params: field_weights: ssn: 0.30 email: 0.25 phone: 0.20 name: 0.15 address: 0.07 city: 0.03 match_threshold: 0.80 review_threshold: 0.55

The plugin reads self.params["field_weights"] at run time and applies them. Change a weight in the YAML, rerun, and your whole matching behavior shifts — no code change. That separation is exactly why the weights are config: tuning is an analyst's job, and it happens far more often than the scoring logic itself changes.

Stage 03

The Composite Score — Combining the Evidence

Now you have raw similarities and weights. The composite score multiplies each field's similarity by its weight, sums the results, and — because the weights sum to 1.0 — lands a single number cleanly between 0 and 1. This is the weighted average, and it's the engine of the whole stage.

The formula, in plain arithmetic

For each field: multiply how similar by how much it matters. That product is the field's contribution. Add up every contribution and you have the composite:

composite = Σ (similarity × weight)
# the abstract pattern composite = sum(similarity[f] * weight[f] for f in fields) # because every weight is a fraction of 1.0, and every # similarity is in [0, 1], the result is automatically in [0, 1]. # no extra normalization needed — that's why weights sum to 1.

Worked example — the same pair, scored properly

Take the overview pair again. This table is the whole computation: every field's similarity, its weight, and the contribution they produce together.

FieldSimilarityWeightContribution (sim × weight)
email1.000.250.250
phone0.000.200.000
name0.920.150.138
address0.740.070.052
city1.000.030.030
ssn(absent)0.30— see Stage 04

*assuming ssn's weight is simply skipped for now — Stage 04 shows why that's not quite right yet.

Notice what the weighting bought you. The phone mismatch (0.00 × 0.20) contributes nothing and drags the score down — correctly, because a disagreeing phone is real counter-evidence. The matching city (1.00 × 0.03) barely registers — correctly, because city agreement is nearly free. The composite reflects the importance-weighted picture, not a flat democracy of fields.

Debug trace — watch the composite accumulate

Here's the same computation as a running tally, the way you'd see it in a debugger stepping field by field:

composite — accumulating contribution by contribution
composite = 0.0 # start
composite → 0.000
+= email: 1.00 × 0.25
composite → 0.250
The strongest single field. A quarter of the maximum possible score, earned in one line, because email is high-weight and a perfect match.
+= phone: 0.00 × 0.20
composite → 0.250
Adds nothing. The disagreeing phone withholds 0.20 of possible score — its absence from the tally is the penalty.
+= name: 0.92 × 0.15
composite → 0.388
A near-perfect name match, but worth only 0.15 at most, so it nudges rather than dominates.
+= address: 0.74 × 0.07  then  += city: 1.00 × 0.03
composite → 0.470
The low-weight fields close out the tally with small contributions — exactly as intended.
Weighted sum vs weighted average — same thing here. Because the weights sum to 1.0, the weighted sum is the weighted average. If your weights didn't sum to 1 (say they were raw importance points like 30, 25, 20...), you'd divide by the total weight at the end to normalize. Keeping weights summing to 1 just bakes that division in up front. Hold that idea — it becomes the key to null handling in the next stage.

Stage 04

Null Handling — The Field That Isn't There

This is the stage that separates a toy scorer from a production one. Real records have missing fields — one side has no SSN, the other has no phone on file. How you treat that gap silently decides thousands of verdicts. Treat it wrong and you'll reject real matches all day without ever seeing why.

The fork: what does a missing field mean?

A field can fail to contribute for two completely different reasons, and conflating them is the entire bug:

Reason 1
Disagreement → 0.0
Both records have a phone, and the phones are different. Similarity is genuinely 0.0. This is real counter-evidence — the field should drag the score down.
Reason 2
Absence → null
One or both records have no phone on file. There's nothing to compare. This isn't evidence of anything — it's the absence of evidence. It should not penalize the pair at all.

The red herring — and what actually decides

This is your classic trap: a missing value and a zero score look identical in the contribution column — both produce 0.0. So the lazy move is to map null to 0.0 and move on. Don't.

✗ Red herring — they look the same
"null and 0.0 both contribute nothing, so treat them the same."

Both put 0.0 in the tally, so why bother distinguishing? Just write sim = sim or 0.0 and the math runs.

The trap: you're looking at the numerator (the contribution), where they really do look alike. But the numerator isn't where the damage is.

✓ What actually decides
The denominator — what you divide by.

A real 0.0 keeps its weight in the denominator: the field was checked, it failed, so the maximum possible score still includes it. The pair is being measured against the full bar.

A null must be removed from the denominator too: the field was never on the table, so it shouldn't count toward the maximum possible score. You measure the pair against a shorter bar.

The rule: a disagreement stays in the denominator (it's a fair test the pair failed). An absence is dropped from both numerator and denominator (it was never a test at all). Concretely: renormalize the weights over only the fields that are actually present.

The same pair, two ways — and the verdict flips

Back to our worked pair. The ssn field is absent — neither record has one on file — but it carries the heaviest weight (0.30). Watch what happens depending on how you handle it.

✗ null treated as 0.0 — full denominator
numerator = 0.470 (email .25 + name .138 + addr .052 + city .03, phone & ssn → 0.0) denominator = 1.00 (all six weights kept) composite = 0.470 / 1.00 = 0.470
verdict
NO MATCH — falls below the 0.55 review line. The pair is rejected.
✓ ssn dropped — renormalized denominator
numerator = 0.470 (ssn contributes nothing AND is removed entirely) denominator = 0.70 (1.00 − 0.30, ssn weight pulled out) composite = 0.470 / 0.70 = 0.671
verdict
REVIEW — clears 0.55. A human gets to look instead of an auto-reject.
This is the silent killer. The naive version doesn't crash. It doesn't log a warning. It just quietly rejects a genuine candidate because a field nobody had was scored as if both sides failed it. On a dataset where SSN is sparsely populated, the null-as-zero bug can suppress a huge fraction of real matches — and you'd never know unless you went looking.

The renormalizing scorer — wrong vs right, with output

✗ null → 0.0
def score(sims, weights): num = 0.0 for f, w in weights.items(): s = sims.get(f) or 0.0 num += s * w # denominator is always 1.0 return num
score(pair_with_absent_ssn)
→ 0.470 ← absent ssn punished the pair
✓ renormalize over present fields
def score(sims, weights): num = 0.0 den = 0.0 for f, w in weights.items(): s = sims.get(f) if s is None: # absent continue # drop from BOTH num += s * w den += w return num / den if den else 0.0
score(pair_with_absent_ssn)
→ 0.671 ← measured against present fields only

The only structural difference is the den accumulator and the continue. The wrong version has a fixed denominator of 1.0; the right version builds the denominator from only the weights of fields that were actually present. One if s is None: continue is the whole fix — and it's the line most homegrown scorers are missing.

Guardrail: if every comparable field is absent, den is 0 and you'd divide by zero. The if den else 0.0 tail handles it — a pair with nothing to compare scores 0.0 and falls out as a non-match, which is the right call.

Stage 05

Thresholds — Turning a Number Into a Verdict

The composite is a number in [0, 1]. A pipeline can't act on a number — it needs a decision. Two thresholds cut the number line into three zones: confident match, confident non-match, and the uncertain middle that goes to a human. This three-way split is what makes the system safe to automate.

Why three bands, not two

The tempting design is one threshold: above it, merge; below it, reject. But that forces a hard call on pairs sitting right at the boundary — exactly the pairs you're least sure about. A single line means your most uncertain decisions are made most confidently. That's backwards.

Three bands give the uncertain middle somewhere to go: a review queue where a person decides. The machine handles the easy 90%; humans handle the genuinely ambiguous 10%.

review_threshold = 0.55 match_threshold = 0.80
NO MATCH · reject
REVIEW · human queue
MATCH · auto-merge
0.00.550.801.0

≥ 0.80MATCHAuto-merge — confident enough to act without review.
0.55 – 0.80REVIEWRoute to a human queue — too uncertain to auto-merge or auto-reject.
< 0.55NO MATCHReject — confident these are different entities.

The classify function

The logic is a two-line ladder. Order matters: check the highest band first, fall through to the middle, default to the lowest.

classify — number to verdict
def classify(composite, match_t=0.80, review_t=0.55): if composite >= match_t: return "match" elif composite >= review_t: return "review" else: return "no_match" classify(0.91) # → "match" classify(0.67) # → "review" classify(0.40) # → "no_match" classify(0.55) # → "review" (boundary is inclusive on the low side)

The lever you're really pulling: precision vs recall

Where you set these two thresholds is a trade-off, and it has a name:

Raise match_threshold (e.g. 0.90)
Higher precision, lower recall

Fewer auto-merges, but the ones you make are almost always correct. You catch fewer true matches (some real ones drop into review), but you make fewer false merges.

Choose this when a wrong merge is expensive — merging two different customers corrupts records, billing, history.

Lower match_threshold (e.g. 0.70)
Higher recall, lower precision

More auto-merges, catching more true matches — but some false ones slip through. You merge aggressively and accept occasional mistakes.

Choose this when a missed match is expensive — duplicate accounts, fractured customer view, the same person counted twice.

🔓
This trade-off — and how to tune the two thresholds against labeled data using precision/recall curves and the size of your review queue — is the whole of Module 05 · Score Aggregation & Confidence. You've just met the lever; next module you learn to set it with evidence instead of a guess.

Stage 06

The Full ScoringPlugin Pattern

Every mechanism so far — weights, weighted sum, null renormalization, three-way classification — assembles into one plugin that runs across millions of candidate pairs in Spark. This is the shape of the ScoringPlugin in your pipeline: config in, a match_decision column out.

The plugin, end to end

Read it top to bottom — every piece is something you built in an earlier stage. The comments mark which stage each block came from.

ScoringPlugin — the assembled pattern (PySpark)
from pyspark.sql import functions as F class ScoringPlugin(BasePlugin): def run(self, df): # df has one row per candidate pair, with a similarity # column per field already computed upstream (Module 02): # sim_ssn, sim_email, sim_phone, sim_name, sim_address, sim_city weights = self.params["field_weights"] # Stage 02 — config match_t = self.params["match_threshold"] # Stage 05 — config review_t = self.params["review_threshold"] # Stage 05 — config # ── Stage 03 + 04: weighted sum with null renormalization ── num = F.lit(0.0) den = F.lit(0.0) for field, w in weights.items(): sim = F.col(f"sim_{field}") present = sim.isNotNull() # Stage 04: absent ≠ 0.0 num = num + F.when(present, sim * w).otherwise(0.0) den = den + F.when(present, F.lit(w)).otherwise(0.0) composite = F.when(den > 0, num / den).otherwise(F.lit(0.0)) df = df.withColumn("composite", composite) # ── Stage 05: classify into three bands ── decision = ( F.when(F.col("composite") >= match_t, F.lit("match")) .when(F.col("composite") >= review_t, F.lit("review")) .otherwise(F.lit("no_match")) ) return df.withColumn("match_decision", decision)

Why it's built this way — the three design choices

Why F.when(...).otherwise(...) instead of a Python if? Because this runs over a whole distributed column at once. You can't write if sim is None on a Spark column — there's no single value to test. F.when(present, ...) is the column-wise version of that if: it evaluates the condition independently for every row, in parallel, across the cluster. It's the exact same null-renormalization logic from Stage 04, lifted from one pair to a billion.

Why build num and den as two separate accumulating columns? Same reason as Stage 04 — the denominator has to reflect only the present fields. By accumulating den with the same present guard as num, an absent field is dropped from both, automatically, per row. A row where SSN is null gets a denominator of 0.70; the row next to it where SSN is present gets 1.00 — and Spark computes both correctly without you branching.

Why read everything from self.params? Because the weights and thresholds are the things analysts tune — frequently — and the scoring logic is the thing engineers change — rarely. Reading them from config keeps the two on separate clocks. An analyst retunes field_weights in YAML; the plugin code never moves.

The shape to remember: config in (weights + thresholds) → one weighted-and-renormalized composite column → one match_decision column out. Everything downstream — clustering in Module 07, the golden record logic from Module 03 — keys off that single match_decision. This plugin is the hinge the whole back half of the pipeline turns on.

Module 08 · MDM / Spark Track · Phase 3 — ER at Scale

Graph Theory & Clustering

Module 07 gave you LSH to find candidate pairs at scale without all-pairs comparison. Module 04/05 gave you a ScoringPlugin and ThresholdTuner that turn each candidate pair into a match/review/no-match verdict. But a verdict is still just a fact about one pair. If A matches B, and B matches C, are A, B, and C one entity, or three separate facts about two entities? This module is the final ER step: turning a pile of pairwise match verdicts into actual master entity clusters, using graph theory — connected components, BFS/DFS, union-find, and the transitive closure that makes clustering necessary in the first place.

What this module adds to your pipeline
Standardize→ Block (Exact + LSH)→ Self-Join→ Field Scoring→ Combine & Classify→ Tune & Calibrate→ Cluster (Graph)→ Golden Record

Why pairwise verdicts aren't enough

Every prior module in Phase 2 and Phase 3 produces pairs: candidate pairs from blocking and LSH, scored pairs from the ScoringPlugin, classified pairs from the ThresholdTuner. None of that machinery ever looks at three records at once. But real customer databases don't split cleanly into pairs — a person who moved twice and changed their phone number each time might match record A↔B, B↔C, but A↔C scores too low to ever surface as a candidate. Treated as isolated facts, you'd merge A with B, and B with C, but never realize all three are the same person unless something forces the transitive leap.

Concept 01
Graph Model
Records become nodes; match verdicts become edges. The entire pairwise output of your pipeline is just one big graph waiting to be traversed.
Concept 02
Connected Components
A cluster is a connected component — every node reachable from every other node by following match edges. This is the formal definition of a "master entity."
Concept 03
BFS/DFS & Union-Find
Two different algorithms to discover the same connected components — one explores the graph node-by-node, the other merges sets incrementally as edges arrive.
Concept 04
Transitive Closure
The reason clustering exists at all: if A matches B and B matches C, A and C end up in the same entity — even if A and C were never directly scored.

Stage 01

From Pairs to Entities — The Moved-Person Problem

Module 04 introduced the moved-person scenario as a reason field weighting matters. Now it's the reason clustering exists: a chain of true matches your scoring pipeline only ever sees two at a time.

The scenario

One real person appears three times in your raw data, once per address they've lived at:

RecordNameAddressPhoneSSN (last 4)
AJon Smith100 Oak St, Austin TX512-555-01014471
BJon Smith200 Pine Ave, Austin TX512-555-01994471
CJonathan Smith200 Pine Ave, Austin TX512-555-01994471

Your ScoringPlugin (Module 04) and ThresholdTuner (Module 06) classify each pair independently:

PairShared fieldsComposite scoreVerdict
A ↔ BSame name, same SSN, different address/phone0.81match
B ↔ CSame address/phone/SSN, name variant (Jon vs Jonathan)0.88match
A ↔ CSame SSN only — different address, different name spelling0.52no-match (below threshold)

A↔C never scores high enough to surface as a match — it might not even have been generated as a candidate pair by blocking or LSH, since the address and phone differ and the name spelling differs too. Looked at as three isolated pairwise facts, you'd be tempted to merge {A, B} into one entity and treat C separately, or merge {B, C} and leave A alone. Either choice loses information you already proved twice over: A=B (via the move) and B=C (via the name normalization). The only correct answer is that all three are the same person, and the only way to get there is to stop thinking pair-by-pair and start thinking about the graph of all match verdicts together.

The pairwise trap: If your pipeline stops at the ThresholdTuner and just writes `(id_a, id_b, verdict)` rows to a table, you have a list of edges — not a list of entities. Two different downstream consumers querying "who is this person's other records" could get two different, inconsistent answers depending on which row they happen to look at first. You need one more step that resolves the whole edge list into a consistent partition of records into entities.

Stage 02

The Graph Model — Nodes, Edges, Components

Everything your pipeline has produced so far — candidate pairs, scores, verdicts — is secretly a graph. Naming it as one is what unlocks the algorithms in this module.

Mapping ER concepts onto graph theory

ER pipeline conceptGraph theory termWhat it is
A raw record (post-standardization)Node / vertexOne point in the graph. Every distinct record_id is a node.
A match verdict between two recordsEdgeAn undirected connection between two nodes. Only `match` verdicts become edges — `review` and `no-match` do not (until a human resolves a review).
A set of records that should become one golden recordConnected componentA maximal set of nodes where every node is reachable from every other node by following edges, directly or through intermediate nodes.
A record with no matches at allSingleton componentA connected component of size 1 — a node with no edges. Still a valid "entity" of one record.
Analogy — the friendship-circle test
Think of match edges like "these two people know each other." If Jon knows Maria, and Maria knows Diego, Jon and Diego are in the same friend circle even if they've never met directly — because there's a path connecting them. A connected component is exactly a friend circle: everyone reachable from everyone else, with no path leading outside the group. Master entity clustering asks the identical question, just with "knows" replaced by "is the same real-world person as."

Worked example — drawing the moved-person graph

Take Stage 01's three records and draw the match edges (A↔B and B↔C are matches; A↔C is not an edge):

NodeEdges toDirectly reachableComponent membership
ABBComponent 1
BA, CA, CComponent 1
CBBComponent 1

Even though A and C have no direct edge, both A and C are reachable from B, and B is reachable from both. By the connected-component definition, {A, B, C} is one component — one master entity — even though only 2 of the 3 possible pairs were ever scored as matches. This is the formal justification for the conclusion Stage 01 reached by inspection: graph connectivity, not pairwise agreement, decides entity membership.

Why this matters operationally: Every "find all records for this person" query, every golden-record merge, every downstream join to this MDM data is implicitly a connected-components query. Getting clustering right is what makes "the same entity" mean the same thing everywhere in your system.

Stage 03

Finding Components by Traversal — BFS & DFS

The most direct way to find connected components: pick an unvisited node, walk every edge reachable from it, and mark everything you touch as one component. Breadth-first and depth-first search are two orderings of the same walk.

The algorithm (component-finding via traversal)

For each node not yet assigned to a component:

  1. Start a new component, add the node to it.
  2. Visit every neighbor via an edge; if unvisited, add it to the same component and queue it for visiting.
  3. Repeat until no more reachable unvisited nodes remain — the component is complete.
  4. Move to the next unassigned node and repeat for the next component.

BFS (breadth-first search) explores all direct neighbors before going further out, using a queue (FIFO). DFS (depth-first search) follows one path as deep as it can before backtracking, using a stack (LIFO) or recursion. Both visit the exact same set of nodes for a connected component — they just visit them in a different order. For component-finding alone, the order doesn't change the answer.

Worked example — a 6-record graph, traced with BFS

Edges from your ScoringPlugin's match verdicts: A-B, B-C, D-E. Record F has no matches.

BFS component trace
Start at A (unvisited). New component_id = 1. Queue: [A]
visited = {A:1}
Pop A. Neighbors of A: [B]. B is unvisited → assign component 1, enqueue.
visited = {A:1, B:1} · queue = [B]
Pop B. Neighbors of B: [A, C]. A already visited (skip). C unvisited → assign component 1, enqueue.
visited = {A:1, B:1, C:1} · queue = [C]
Pop C. Neighbors of C: [B]. Already visited. Queue empty → component 1 complete: {A, B, C}.
components so far = [{A,B,C}]
Next unvisited node: D. New component_id = 2. Traverse D→E (same pattern). Component 2 complete: {D, E}.
components so far = [{A,B,C}, {D,E}]
Next unvisited node: F. No neighbors. Component 3 = {F} (singleton).
F never matched anything, but it's still a valid entity of one record — every node ends up in exactly one component, including isolated ones.

Final result

Component IDRecordsSizeMaster entity
1A, B, C3One merged golden record
2D, E2One merged golden record
3F1Unchanged — its own entity
Cost characteristic: BFS/DFS visits every node once and every edge once — $O(N + E)$ total. That's the right complexity, but it assumes the whole graph fits comfortably in memory on one machine for the traversal to walk. At MDM scale (millions of nodes, an unstructured edge list scattered across a cluster), a single-machine, single-threaded BFS/DFS over the raw adjacency list is the wrong tool — it doesn't parallelize naturally the way a Spark job needs to. That gap is exactly why union-find (Stage 04) and GraphFrames (Stage 07) exist.

Stage 04

Union-Find — Merging Sets Incrementally

Instead of walking the graph from scratch, union-find processes edges one at a time, incrementally merging the sets each edge connects. It never needs the whole adjacency list in memory at once, which is why it's the standard approach for streaming or distributed clustering.

The core idea: every node starts as its own component

Union-find (a.k.a. "disjoint-set union") tracks a parent pointer for every node. Initially every node points to itself — every node is its own component of size 1. Processing an edge `(x, y)` means: "find the representative (root) of x's component, find the representative of y's component, and if they differ, merge the two components by pointing one root at the other."

Operation 1
find(x)
Follow x's parent pointer up the chain until you reach a node that points to itself — that's the component's root/representative.
Operation 2
union(x, y)
find(x) and find(y); if the roots differ, set one root's parent to the other root. The two components are now one.

Worked example — the same 6-record graph, traced with union-find

Edges arrive in this order: A-B, D-E, B-C. Record F never appears in an edge.

union-find trace
Initialize: every node is its own parent.
parent = {A:A, B:B, C:C, D:D, E:E, F:F}
Process edge A-B. find(A)=A, find(B)=B. Roots differ → union: parent[B] = A.
parent = {A:A, B:A, C:C, D:D, E:E, F:F}
A and B are now in the same component, rooted at A.
Process edge D-E. find(D)=D, find(E)=E. Roots differ → union: parent[E] = D.
parent = {A:A, B:A, C:C, D:D, E:D, F:F}
Process edge B-C. find(B): B→A (root A). find(C)=C. Roots differ → union: parent[C] = A.
parent = {A:A, B:A, C:A, D:D, E:D, F:F}
C joins A and B's component without ever having a direct edge to A — this is transitive closure happening automatically, one union at a time.
No more edges. F's parent is still itself.
Final roots: find(A)=A, find(B)=A, find(C)=A, find(D)=D, find(E)=D, find(F)=F
Group every node by its root: {A,B,C} (root A), {D,E} (root D), {F} (root F) — identical result to the BFS trace in Stage 03.
Why union-find fits distributed pipelines better than BFS/DFS: Union-find processes edges as a stream, in any order, without needing the full adjacency structure in memory — it only needs a parent-pointer map, which can be represented as a Spark DataFrame and updated iteratively. BFS/DFS needs to actively walk from node to node, which requires random-access lookups that don't map cleanly onto Spark's columnar, distributed execution model. This is the real reason GraphFrames' connectedComponents() (Stage 07) is implemented as a union-find-style iterative label-propagation algorithm under the hood, not a literal distributed BFS.

Path compression — keeping find() fast

In a naive union-find, long chains of parent pointers (A→B→C→D→...) make `find()` slow over many unions. Path compression fixes this: every time `find(x)` walks up to the root, it re-points every node along the way directly to the root, flattening the chain for future lookups. Combined with union-by-size (always attaching the smaller component's root to the larger one), this gives near-constant-time operations even on millions of edges — the textbook reason union-find scales where naive traversal doesn't.

Stage 05 · The Climax

Transitive Closure — The Gotcha That Justifies This Whole Module

Both algorithms above quietly did something that pairwise scoring never could: they merged records that were never directly compared. This is transitive closure, and it cuts both ways — it's the entire point of clustering, and also its biggest risk.

The red herring: "clustering just groups the matched pairs"

✗ Red Herring — the naive mental model
"Clustering takes the match pairs and bundles each one into a group."

This makes it sound like clustering only ever combines pairs that were already proven to match by your ScoringPlugin — as if it's just a bookkeeping step that relabels existing verdicts.

Under this model, you'd expect every pair inside a cluster to have a high composite score. That assumption is false, and believing it leads directly to data-quality incidents.

✓ What actually happens
Clustering creates new "facts" — pairs that were never scored at all.

If A↔B is a match and B↔C is a match, A and C land in the same cluster even if A↔C was never a candidate pair, never scored, and would score below threshold if it were. The cluster asserts A and C are the same entity — a claim your ScoringPlugin never made and never checked.

This is mathematically correct and exactly how the moved-person scenario (Stage 01) gets resolved. But it is also exactly how an over-eager clustering step silently merges unrelated people through a single weak bridge match.

The failure mode: a single weak edge merges a crowd

Suppose your blocking/LSH/scoring pipeline produces a borderline `review`-band match between two genuinely different people who happen to share a common, generic name and a shared apartment complex address (a classic high-density-address false positive). If that review pair is mistakenly auto-approved (or your threshold band is set too loosely), it becomes one edge — and that single edge can fuse two entire pre-existing clusters into one giant, wrong entity:

Before the bad edgeAfter adding 1 bad edge (X↔Y)Consequence
Cluster 1: {Maria G., 4 records, same household}One merged cluster: 7 records, two unrelated familiesGolden record now blends two people's SSNs, phones, and addresses into one "entity" — a serious data-quality and possibly compliance incident.
Cluster 2: {Maria G., 3 records, different family, same apartment complex}
The bridge-edge trap: The damage from one wrong match scales with cluster size, not with how confident that one match was. A 60%-confidence match between two singleton records merges 2 records into a wrong entity. The same 60%-confidence match between two existing 10-record clusters merges 20 records into one wrong entity. Clustering amplifies every scoring mistake by the size of the clusters it touches. This is why threshold tuning (Module 06) and clustering (this module) cannot be reasoned about independently — a threshold that looked "safe enough" in pairwise precision/recall terms can still be unsafe once you account for what it does when it becomes a bridge edge between two large existing clusters.

The mitigation: cluster-level sanity checks

Because transitive closure can silently merge a crowd, production clustering steps add a guard the pairwise ScoringPlugin never needed: after computing connected components, re-examine any cluster above a size threshold (e.g. >5 records) and check whether it is internally consistent — do all records in the cluster pairwise agree on enough core fields (SSN, DOB) to plausibly be one person? A cluster that only holds together through one or two bridge edges, with otherwise dissimilar members, is a strong signal of a bad bridge match and should be flagged for review rather than auto-merged.

Stage 06

Choosing an Approach — BFS/DFS vs Union-Find vs GraphFrames

All three approaches find the same connected components. The choice between them is about scale and execution environment, not correctness.

QUESTION 1
Does the whole edge list fit comfortably in memory on one machine?
Yes → BFS/DFS (or a simple in-memory union-find) is fine — e.g. a one-off analysis script, a small reference dataset, or debugging a sample of records pulled out of the pipeline. No (millions+ of records/edges, data lives in a Spark DataFrame) → go to Question 2.
QUESTION 2
Are you inside a Spark pipeline already, with edges as a DataFrame of `(id_a, id_b)` pairs?
Yes → use GraphFrames' connectedComponents() (Stage 07) — it's a distributed, union-find-style label-propagation implementation built specifically for this. Don't hand-roll a distributed BFS. No, you're prototyping in plain Python/pandas → a hand-rolled union-find with path compression is simple, fast, and easy to unit test before porting the logic's intent into the Spark job.
QUESTION 3
Do you need to inspect the traversal path (e.g. "show me the chain of matches that connected A to C") for audit/debugging, not just the final grouping?
Yes → BFS/DFS naturally gives you the path as a byproduct of the traversal order. Union-find's parent pointers can reconstruct a path too, but it takes an extra step (walking parent chains for both nodes back to their common root). No, you only need the final cluster assignment → either works; pick based on Questions 1–2.

Side-by-side summary

ApproachBest forComplexityDistributes well?
BFS/DFSSmall in-memory graphs, debugging, audit-path reconstruction$O(N + E)$No — needs random-access node lookups
Union-find (hand-rolled)Prototyping, unit tests, small-to-medium edge streams~$O(E \log^* N)$ with path compressionPartially — edges can stream in any order, but the parent map is still one structure

Stage 07

The Production Pattern — GraphFrames in PySpark

GraphFrames (a Spark package) gives you a distributed connectedComponents() that takes exactly the edge list your hybrid candidate pipeline (Module 07) and ScoringPlugin/ThresholdTuner (Modules 04–05) already produce.

The input shape GraphFrames expects

GraphFrames needs two DataFrames: vertices with an id column, and edges with src and dst columns. Your pipeline already has both — vertices are your standardized records, edges are the rows from your ThresholdTuner that classified as `match`.

GraphFrames connected components pattern
from graphframes import GraphFrame from pyspark.sql import functions as F # 1. Vertices: every standardized record is a node vertices = standardized_df.select(F.col("record_id").alias("id")) # 2. Edges: only the pairs your ThresholdTuner classified as "match" # (matches Module 06's classify() output, filtered to the match band) edges = scored_pairs_df.filter( F.col("verdict") == "match" ).select( F.col("id_a").alias("src"), F.col("id_b").alias("dst") ) # 3. Build the graph and run connected components # GraphFrames requires a checkpoint dir for the iterative algorithm spark.sparkContext.setCheckpointDir("/tmp/checkpoints") g = GraphFrame(vertices, edges) components_df = g.connectedComponents() # 4. Result: every record_id now has a "component" id (the cluster id) components_df.show() # +---------+-----------+ # | id | component | # +---------+-----------+ # | A | 17179869184| # | B | 17179869184| # | C | 17179869184| # | D | 17179869185| # | E | 17179869185| # | F | 17179869186| # +---------+-----------+
The checkpoint-dir trap: connectedComponents() runs an iterative algorithm internally (multiple rounds of label propagation until labels stop changing). Without a checkpoint directory, Spark's lazy evaluation builds an ever-growing lineage graph across iterations and the job will eventually fail or stall on lineage recomputation. Always call setCheckpointDir() before invoking connectedComponents() — this is the single most common GraphFrames production bug.

Joining components back to build golden records

The `component` column is just an opaque cluster id — join it back onto your standardized data, then group by it to drive the golden-record selection logic from Module 03's window functions:

cluster → golden record handoff
# Attach the cluster id to every standardized record clustered_df = standardized_df.join( components_df, standardized_df.record_id == components_df.id ).drop("id") # Module 03's window functions now operate per-cluster instead of per # source-record: rank by recency within each component to pick the # golden record's field values. from pyspark.sql.window import Window w = Window.partitionBy("component").orderBy(F.col("updated_at").desc()) golden_df = clustered_df.withColumn("rn", F.row_number().over(w)) \ .filter(F.col("rn") == 1)
Why this completes the pipeline: `component` is the column that finally turns "a pile of pairwise verdicts" into "one row per real-world entity." Everything from Module 00 onward — blocking, scoring, thresholds, LSH — exists to produce a trustworthy edge list. This module's `connectedComponents()` call is the step that converts that edge list into the partition of records your golden-record logic actually needs.

Honest Gaps
What this module didn't cover
Weighted/probabilistic clustering: Real production systems sometimes weight edges by match confidence and use community-detection algorithms (e.g. label propagation with edge weights, or graph cuts) instead of treating every match edge as equally binding. We treated all match edges as equally strong, which is the simpler and more common starting point but not the only approach.

Cluster-splitting / cluster review workflows: We named the bridge-edge mitigation (flag oversized clusters for review) but didn't build the actual review-queue logic or a "split this cluster" operational tool — that's a natural follow-on to Module 06's review queue concept, applied at the cluster level instead of the pair level.

GraphFrames internals: We used connectedComponents() as a black box. The actual algorithm (a distributed variant of Hash-to-Min / Alternating-update label propagation) and its convergence/iteration-count behavior at scale weren't traced.

Graph algorithms beyond connectivity: Centrality measures, shortest-path, or community detection beyond connected components — useful for fraud-ring detection or relationship graphs, but out of scope for straightforward entity-resolution clustering.

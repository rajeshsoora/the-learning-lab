Graph Theory & Clustering

Everything before this module ends the same way: with a pile of slips of paper, each one saying "record A and record B are the same person." Blocking found the candidate pairs. The ScoringPlugin scored them. The ThresholdTuner stamped each one match, review, or no-match. But a pile of "these two are the same" slips is not yet a list of people — and it is definitely not yet a list of households. This whole module answers one question.

The one question this module answers
You're handed a shoebox of pairwise "same person" slips. How do you turn that shoebox into a stable directory of real people — and the households they live in — that stays consistent every single time the pipeline re-runs? Hold onto that question. Every stage below is one step toward it, and each step exists only because the previous step left something unfinished.

We'll keep one picture in mind the whole way through — a town census. Each record is one census form someone filled out. A match slip means "these two forms describe the same resident." First we'll group forms into residents (Stages 01–06). Then we'll give every resident a name badge that survives next year's census (Stage 07). Then we'll group residents who live under one roof into households (Stage 08). Same picture, start to finish.

Where this module sits — and the three new things it adds (amber)
Standardize→ Block + LSH→ Score→ Classify→ Cluster (Graph)→ Entity ID→ Households→ Golden Record

Three amber boxes, three movements in this module. Cluster turns the slips into people (the graph half). Entity ID gives each person a durable name. Households groups people who belong together but aren't the same person. Here's the shape of each before we start:

Movement 01 · Stages 01–06
Slips → People
Treat the slips as a graph: records are dots, match slips are lines connecting them. A "person" is a clump of dots all reachable from each other. Two algorithms find the clumps; one gotcha makes the whole thing dangerous.
Movement 02 · Stage 07
People → Stable Names
The clustering step names each person with a throwaway number that changes every run. We replace it with a fingerprint computed from who's in the clump — so the same person keeps the same ID forever, across runs and across source systems.
Movement 03 · Stage 08
People → Households
A second clustering pass — but the dots are now people, not records, and a line means "same roof," not "same person." Same machinery, different rules, run on top of the first result.

Stage 01

A Verdict Only Ever Talks About Two Records

Your scoring pipeline is powerful, but it has a blind spot built into its shape: it looks at exactly two records at a time, forever. Watch what that costs you the moment a real person shows up three times.

One person, three forms

Here's a single real person — call her the resident — who filled out a census form once at each address she ever lived at. Three forms, one human:

RecordNameAddressPhoneSSN (last 4)
AJon Smith100 Oak St, Austin TX512-555-01014471

CJonathan Smith200 Pine Ave, Austin TX512-555-01994471

Your ScoringPlugin (Module 04) and ThresholdTuner (Module 06) only ever see two forms side by side. So they produce three independent verdicts:

PairWhat they shareScoreVerdict
A ↔ BSame name & SSN, different address/phone (she moved)0.81match

A ↔ CSame SSN only — different address AND name spelling0.52no-match

Look at the highlighted row. A and C are the same person — but the only thing they still share is the SSN, because between form A and form C she both moved and changed how she wrote her name. So the pair A↔C scores 0.52 and gets stamped no-match. It may not even have been generated as a candidate pair in the first place.

Now you're stuck. You have proof A=B. You have proof B=C. But the pipeline told you A≠C. If you trust the verdicts literally and pair them up, you'd merge {A,B} and separately {B,C} — and never realize all three are one human. The truth (A, B, C are one person) is a fact no single pairwise verdict can ever state, because stating it requires looking at all three at once.

This is the gap that creates this entire module. A pile of (id_a, id_b, verdict) slips is a list of connections, not a list of people. Two parts of your system could ask "what are all the records for this person?" and get two different answers depending on which slip they read first. Something has to take the whole pile at once and resolve it into one clean, agreed-upon grouping. To do that, we need to stop seeing slips — and start seeing a shape.

Stage 02

So What Is the Shape Hiding in the Pile?

Stage 01 said "look at the whole pile at once" — but a pile has no structure to look at. The trick is to notice the slips were secretly drawing a picture all along. Name the picture, and a hundred years of mathematics suddenly applies to your customer data.

Analogy — the friendship map
Forget records for a second. Imagine a room of people, and you draw a line between any two who know each other. Now ask: who's in Jon's circle of friends? Not just the people Jon directly knows — also the friends of his friends, and theirs, as far as the lines reach. If Jon knows Maria, and Maria knows Diego, then Jon and Diego are in the same circle even though they've never met. A circle is everyone you can reach by walking along the lines. That's the whole idea. Entity resolution asks the identical question — just swap "knows" for "is the same person as."

The three words that make it precise

The friendship map has exactly three pieces, and each one is just a record-pipeline thing wearing a math name:

In the friendship map……the math name is…and in your pipeline it's
A person (a dot)Node (or vertex)One record. Every distinct record_id is one dot.
A "they know each other" lineEdgeOne match verdict. Only match slips become lines — review and no-match draw nothing.

A loner who knows nobodySingleton componentA record that matched nothing. Still a valid person — a circle of one.

That middle row is the quiet, important one: a connected component is "everyone reachable by walking the lines," not "everyone directly connected." That single word — reachable — is what's about to rescue our moved-person.

Drawing Stage 01's person as a shape

Take A, B, C from Stage 01. We proved two lines: A–B and B–C. (A–C scored no-match, so there's no line there.) Draw it:

There it is. A and C have no line between them — but A can reach C by walking A→B→C. So all three sit in one circle: one person, even though only 2 of the 3 possible pairs ever scored as a match. The thing Stage 01 could only point at ("surely they're the same"), the graph now proves by a rule: connectivity decides membership, not pairwise agreement.

Why this is the load-bearing idea of the module: every "show me all records for this customer" query, every golden-record merge, every downstream join is secretly asking "what circle is this dot in?" Get that right and "the same entity" means the same thing everywhere in your company. Now there's only one thing missing — we have a definition of a circle, but no procedure for finding one. A computer can't "just see" the circle the way your eye did. That's Stage 03.

Stage 03

Your Eye Saw the Circle Instantly. A Computer Can't.

"Everyone reachable by walking the lines" is a perfect definition and a useless instruction — a machine needs a literal recipe. The most natural one is exactly what you'd do by hand: pick a dot, and physically walk every line out of it, marking everything you touch.

The recipe — walk until you can't

To find one circle: stand on an unvisited dot, then keep walking outward along lines, painting every dot you reach with the same color, until there's nowhere new to go.

  1. Stand on a dot nobody's visited yet. Start a new circle; paint this dot.
  2. Walk each line out of it. Any dot on the far end that isn't painted — paint it the same color, and add it to your "still to walk from" list.
  3. Keep going until your list is empty. That circle is now complete.
  4. Find any still-unpainted dot and repeat for the next circle.

There are two flavors of this walk, and the only difference is the order you walk in. BFS (breadth-first) fans out — it visits all your immediate neighbors before going deeper, working in rings outward. DFS (depth-first) plunges — it follows one path as far as it goes, then backtracks. For finding circles, they give the identical answer; they just paint the dots in a different order. So pick whichever's easier to write.

Watch it run — a 6-record town, traced step by step

Six records this time. The match slips drew these lines: A–B, B–C, D–E. Record F matched nobody. Let's walk it with BFS and watch the circles fall out:

painted = {A:1, B:1, C:1} · to-walk = [C]

Notice C got painted because of B, never because of a direct A–C line — the walk found the moved-person automatically.

circles so far = [{A,B,C}, {D,E}]
Next unpainted dot: F. No lines at all. Circle 3 = {F}.
F matched no one, but it still becomes its own circle of one — every dot lands in exactly one circle, loners included.

So why isn't this the end of the module? This walk is correct and even efficient on paper — it touches each dot once and each line once. But read the recipe again: "walk from this dot to that dot" means constantly jumping around the graph, looking up "who are this dot's neighbors?" on demand. That's fine when all six records sit in your laptop's memory. It falls apart when there are 50 million records and the lines are scattered in pieces across a hundred machines in a Spark cluster — there's no single place to "stand" and "walk" from. We need a method that never has to hold the whole map at once. That's Stage 04.

Stage 04

A Method That Never Holds the Whole Map

The walk needed to roam the graph. Union-find refuses to roam. It looks at the match slips one at a time, in any order, and just keeps a running answer to a single question: "what's the name of this dot's circle right now?" That tiny shift is what lets it run across a hundred machines.

Analogy — name tags and "ask your leader"
Everyone in the room starts wearing a name tag that just says their own name — everyone is their own group leader. Now you read match slips one at a time. Each slip says "these two are in the same group." To merge them, you ask each person "who's your leader?" (they point to whoever their tag names, who points to their leader, until you reach someone whose tag names themselves — that's the real leader). If the two leaders are different people, you tell one leader: "from now on, your leader is the other one." One pointer change, and two whole groups are now one. You never had to gather everybody — you only ever touched the two people on the slip and their leaders.

The two moves, named

Move 1
find(x) — "who's your leader?"
Follow x's pointer to whoever it names, then theirs, and so on, until you reach someone who points to themselves. That person is the circle's leader (its "root").
Move 2
union(x, y) — "you're one group now"
find the leader of x and the leader of y. If they're different, point one leader at the other. The two circles are now a single circle, in one step.

The same 6-record town — but processed slip by slip

Same town as Stage 03. The slips arrive in this order: A–B, D–E, B–C. F never appears on any slip. Watch the leader pointers update:

union-find — one slip at a time
Start: everyone is their own leader.
leader = {A→A, B→B, C→C, D→D, E→E, F→F}
Slip A–B. Leader of A is A; leader of B is B. Different → point B's leader at A.
leader = {A→A, B→A, C→C, D→D, E→E, F→F}
A and B are now one circle, led by A.
Slip D–E. Leader of D is D; leader of E is E. Different → point E's leader at D.
leader = {A→A, B→A, C→C, D→D, E→D, F→F}
Slip B–C. Ask B's leader: B→A, so A. Ask C's leader: C. Different → point C's leader at A.
leader = {A→A, B→A, C→A, D→D, E→D, F→F}
C joined A's circle without any A–C slip ever existing — the moved-person got resolved again, this time one pointer at a time.
No more slips. Group everyone by their final leader.
{A,B,C} led by A · {D,E} led by D · {F} led by F
Identical answer to the BFS walk in Stage 03 — same circles, completely different method.
Why this one survives a Spark cluster and the walk didn't: union-find eats slips as a stream, in any order, holding nothing but the leader pointers — a structure that's just a table of (node → its leader), exactly the kind of thing a Spark DataFrame is built to update over and over. The walk needed to chase neighbors around the graph (random jumps); union-find only ever pokes at the two dots on the current slip. This is the real reason the production tool in Stage 06 is a union-find-style algorithm under the hood, not a giant distributed walk.

One speed trick: keep the leader chains short

If you're not careful, "ask your leader, who asks their leader, who asks theirs…" can grow into a long chain, and every find crawls the whole thing. Path compression fixes it cheaply: every time you trace a chain up to the real leader, you re-point everyone you passed straight at that leader. Next time, they answer in one hop. Combined with "always attach the smaller group under the bigger one's leader," this keeps every operation effectively constant-time, even over millions of slips — the textbook reason union-find scales where the walk chokes.

Stage 05 · The Turn

Both Methods Just Merged Records Nobody Ever Compared

Did you catch what happened twice? In both the BFS walk and the union-find pass, C ended up in A's circle — and A–C was never scored, never even a candidate. That "reach through the middle" is the entire point of clustering. It's also the most dangerous thing in your pipeline. Same mechanism, both faces.

The comforting story that's wrong

✗ The tempting belief
"Clustering just bundles up the matches my scorer already found."

This makes clustering sound like harmless filing — it only ever groups pairs your ScoringPlugin already proved match, so every pair inside a circle must be a strong, checked match.

If that were true, clustering could never surprise you. It would just relabel verdicts you already trusted.

✓ What actually happens
Clustering invents brand-new "facts" your scorer never checked.

When A–B and B–C are matches, A and C land in one circle even though A–C was never scored, and would score below threshold if it had been. The circle now asserts "A and C are the same person" — a claim no verdict ever made.

This is exactly how the moved-person gets correctly reunited. It is also exactly how one bad match can quietly swallow a crowd.

This "reach through the middle" has a name: transitive closure. If A connects to B and B connects to C, the circle silently adds the A–C connection for you. Wonderful when B is a true bridge. Catastrophic when B is a mistake.

The failure mode — one weak line swallows two crowds

Suppose two genuinely different people share a generic name ("Maria G.") and live in the same big apartment complex. Your pipeline coughs up a borderline review-band slip between them — and someone (or a too-loose threshold) lets that one slip through as a line. Watch what a single bad line does when it lands between two existing circles:

Before the bad lineAfter adding ONE bad line (X–Y)The damage
Circle 1: Maria G. — 4 records, one real familyOne fused circle: 7 records, two unrelated families, treated as a single personThe golden record now blends two people's SSNs, phones, and addresses into one "entity" — a serious data-quality and possibly compliance incident.
Circle 2: Maria G. — 3 records, a different family, same complex
The bridge-edge trap — the one sentence to carry out of this module: the damage from a wrong match scales with the size of the circles it touches, not with how confident that one match was. A 60%-confidence mistake between two loners corrupts 2 records. The same 60% mistake between two 10-record circles corrupts 20. Clustering takes every scoring error and multiplies it by the size of whatever it bridges. This is precisely why Module 06's threshold tuning and this module can't be reasoned about separately — a threshold that looked "safe enough" in pairwise precision terms can be quietly unsafe the instant it becomes a bridge between two big circles.

The guard the pairwise world never needed

Because transitive closure can fuse a crowd in silence, production clustering adds a check the ScoringPlugin never had to: after the circles form, re-examine any unusually large one and ask "do these members actually agree with each other on core fields — SSN, DOB — enough to plausibly be one person?" A big circle that only holds together through one or two thin bridge lines, with members who otherwise look nothing alike, is the fingerprint of a bad bridge. Flag it for a human instead of auto-merging. You'll get hands on exactly this fusing-of-two-circles moment in the live widget at Stage 09.

Stage 06

Which of These Do You Actually Run in Production?

You now know two methods plus the gotcha they share. In a real Spark MDM job, which one runs — and how? The honest answer is "neither, by hand" — at scale you reach for a library that does the union-find idea for you, distributed. Let's pick deliberately, then run it.

The three-question pick

QUESTION 1
Does the whole pile of lines fit comfortably in one machine's memory?
Yes (a one-off script, a small reference set, debugging a sample) → a plain in-memory BFS/DFS or union-find is totally fine. No (millions of records, lines living in a Spark DataFrame) → go to Question 2.
QUESTION 2
Are you already inside a Spark pipeline, with lines as a DataFrame of (id_a, id_b) pairs?
Yes → use GraphFrames' connectedComponents() (below). It's a distributed, union-find-style algorithm built for exactly this. Do not hand-roll a distributed walk. No, you're prototyping in plain Python → a hand-rolled union-find with path compression is simple, fast, and easy to unit-test before you port its intent into the Spark job.
QUESTION 3
Do you need to show the chain — "here's the path of matches that connected A to C" — for audit or debugging?
Yes → BFS/DFS hands you the path for free as a byproduct of the walk. No, you only need the final grouping → use whatever Questions 1–2 pointed you at.
ApproachBest forDistributes across a cluster?
BFS / DFS walkSmall in-memory graphs, debugging, showing the audit pathNo — needs random jumps around the graph
Union-find (hand-rolled)Prototyping, unit tests, small-to-medium streams of linesPartly — eats lines in any order, but the leader table is one structure
GraphFrames connectedComponents()Production MDM on millions+ of recordsYes — built for it

Running it — GraphFrames wants two tables

GraphFrames asks for a vertices table (an id column — your records) and an edges table (src, dst columns — your match lines). You already produce both:

connected components in PySpark
from graphframes import GraphFrame from pyspark.sql import functions as F # 1. Vertices: every standardized record is a dot vertices = standardized_df.select(F.col("record_id").alias("id")) # 2. Edges: ONLY the pairs the ThresholdTuner stamped "match" edges = scored_pairs_df.filter( F.col("verdict") == "match" ).select( F.col("id_a").alias("src"), F.col("id_b").alias("dst") ) # 3. REQUIRED: give Spark a scratch dir before running (see trap below) spark.sparkContext.setCheckpointDir("/tmp/checkpoints") g = GraphFrame(vertices, edges) components_df = g.connectedComponents() components_df.show() # +----+-------------+ # | id | component | <- the circle id (one per person) # +----+-------------+ # | A | 17179869184 | # | B | 17179869184 | A, B, C share a component => one entity # | C | 17179869184 | # | D | 17179869185 | # | E | 17179869185 | # | F | 17179869186 | F is its own singleton # +----+-------------+
The one bug everyone hits — the checkpoint dir: connectedComponents() works by repeating a round of "everyone tell your neighbors your current circle label" over and over until labels stop changing. Without a checkpoint directory, Spark's lazy evaluation keeps stacking the whole history of those rounds into an ever-growing plan, and the job eventually stalls or dies recomputing it. Calling setCheckpointDir() first lets Spark snip that history between rounds. Forget it and your job hangs — it's the single most common GraphFrames production failure.

The handoff to golden records — and the catch hiding in that output

That component column is the payoff: join it back onto your records, group by it, and Module 03's window functions pick the best field values per circle to build the golden record:

component → golden record
clustered_df = standardized_df.join( components_df, standardized_df.record_id == components_df.id ).drop("id") # Module 03's window, now per-circle: newest record wins each field from pyspark.sql.window import Window w = Window.partitionBy("component").orderBy(F.col("updated_at").desc()) golden_df = clustered_df.withColumn("rn", F.row_number().over(w)) \ .filter(F.col("rn") == 1)
But look hard at that component number: 17179869184. Where did it come from? Not from who is in the circle — it's a label GraphFrames cooked up from the internal order it happened to process partitions and vertices in, this run. It's the circle's answer to "what's your name?", and that name is an accident of how the job ran today. Save it downstream as "customer #17179869184," re-run the pipeline tomorrow with even one new record in the mix, and that exact same trio of people can come back wearing a completely different number. You haven't actually named your people yet — you've tagged them with disappearing ink. Fixing that is Stage 07.

Stage 07 · New ground

Your Entity Is Named 17179869184 — and Won't Be Tomorrow

The circles are correct. Their names are garbage — temporary, run-specific numbers that reshuffle every time the job runs. A directory whose entries get renumbered each night is useless to everything downstream. We need a name that's computed from who the person is, so it never changes.

Analogy — the census badge that survives next year
Picture the census again. This year the office groups the forms into residents correctly, then numbers the residents 1, 2, 3… in the order it happened to walk the streets. Next year it walks a different route — so "resident 1" is now a totally different family. Anyone who wrote down "resident 1" last year is now pointing at a stranger. The fix isn't a better counter. It's to stop counting at all and instead give each resident a badge whose number is computed from who's in the household on the form — same people in, same badge out, forever, no matter what order anyone walked the streets. That computed-from-the-contents badge is a deterministic hash.

What "deterministic" buys you

A hash function is a meat grinder for text: feed it a string, it spits out a fixed-length scramble (say, 64 hex characters). Two properties are all we need, and they're the whole trick:

Property 1 · same in → same out
Deterministic
The same input text always produces the exact same scramble — on any machine, any day, any run. No randomness, no counter, no run-order. This is what makes the badge stable.
Property 2 · different in → different out
Distinct
Two different inputs land on two different scrambles (for all practical purposes). So two different people get two different badges automatically — no coordination needed.

So the recipe writes itself: build the badge out of the circle's membership. Take the record_ids in the circle, lay them in a fixed order, glue them into one string, and hash it. Because the badge is computed only from who's inside, the same people always yield the same badge — the run-order that poisoned 17179869184 never enters the calculation.

The fixed-order step is the one people skip — and it's load-bearing

Here's the subtle part. The members of a circle don't arrive in a guaranteed order — one run might hand you [C, A, B], another [A, B, C]. If you hash them as-is, you'd get two different badges for the same circle, and you're right back to unstable names. So you sort first. Sorting makes [C,A,B] and [A,B,C] both collapse to the identical string before hashing:

Building it in PySpark

Four small idioms, each doing one job. Read the subgoal note under each before the line, so no piece is a mystery:

deterministic entity_id from a circle's members
from pyspark.sql import functions as F # start from Stage 06's output: (id = record, component = circle label this run) entity_map = ( components_df # JOB: gather every record_id in a circle into ONE list per circle .groupBy("component") .agg(F.collect_list("id").alias("members")) # JOB: put that list in a FIXED order so {C,A,B} and {A,B,C} match .withColumn("members_sorted", F.array_sort("members")) # JOB: glue the sorted ids into one string, separated by "|" .withColumn("member_key", F.concat_ws("|", "members_sorted")) # JOB: grind that string into a stable 64-char fingerprint = the badge .withColumn("entity_id", F.sha2(F.col("member_key"), 256)) ) # explode back to the durable artifact: one row per record -> its entity_id record_to_entity = ( entity_map .select("entity_id", F.explode("members_sorted").alias("record_id")) )
One Spark caveat for the table above: the trace shows the logical result — one badge per circle. Spark doesn't compute these rows top-to-bottom like a person reading a list; it applies sha2 across the whole member_key column at once, in parallel, across the cluster. The order you read the rows in is not the order Spark runs them — only the per-circle values are what's guaranteed.

That last table — record_to_entity, one row per record pointing at its entity_id — is the real prize of this stage. It's the durable record → entity mapping every downstream system joins against. Opaque run-specific labels never leave this module; stable fingerprints do.

The catch — and the genuine fork in the road

Hashing the member set is beautifully simple and completely stateless. But it has a sharp edge: the badge is computed from exactly who's in the circle, so the moment membership changes, the badge changes. Add one newly-discovered record to A's circle next month, and "A|B|C" becomes "A|B|C|D2" — a different string, a different badge. The person didn't change, but their ID just did, and everything downstream that saved the old badge is now orphaned. So there are two real strategies, and which one you want depends on what you're promising downstream:

Option A — hash the member set
Stateless. The ID is the membership.

Win: dead simple, no history to store, the same circle anywhere computes the same ID with zero coordination. Great for a snapshot, an export, or a join key within a single run.

Edge: add or remove one record and the entity "renames." Bad if downstream systems persist the ID and expect it to outlive membership changes.

Option B — persist an ID map
Stateful. The ID follows the person across runs.

How: keep last run's record → entity_id table. Each new run, match new circles to old ones by membership overlap; if a new circle overlaps an existing entity, reuse that entity_id and just add the new records. Mint a brand-new ID only for a genuinely new person.

Win: "customer #X" stays #X even as records get added — exactly what a CRM or warehouse foreign key needs.

Cross-cluster / cross-run dedup is the same overlap idea, one level up. Imagine two source systems each clustered their own records and minted their own entity_ids — and the same real person exists in both. Now you have two different IDs for one human. You reconcile them exactly the way Option B reconciles runs: line the two ID maps up, find entities whose members (or strongly-matching keys like SSN) overlap, and collapse them onto one canonical entity_id in a master map. Whether the duplicate came from "yesterday's run" or "the other system," the cure is the same — match by overlap, keep one ID, retire the other.

Either way, your people now carry names that mean something stable. Which means we can finally do something we couldn't before: treat a whole person as a single dot and start grouping people — not records — into something bigger. That's Stage 08.

Stage 08 · New ground

You Found Individuals. You Still Haven't Found Households.

Two things are still unfinished. First, the whole clustering pass only used the confident match lines — it quietly threw away every review-band slip. Second, and bigger: people who are not the same person can still belong together — a family under one roof. Resolving that needs the same machinery again, but pointed at a completely different relationship.

The leftover slips — possible-match clustering

Remember Module 06's three bands: match, review, no-match. Stage 06 only turned match slips into lines, on purpose — you do not want shaky review-band guesses fusing your confident entities (that's the bridge-edge nightmare from Stage 05, invited in deliberately). But those review slips aren't trash; they're "maybe duplicates" worth a second, separate look:

  • Run a second clustering pass over only the review-band lines — kept completely apart from the trusted entity graph. The circles it forms are "possible duplicate groups," surfaced to a human review queue, never auto-merged into the real entities.
  • Or, after a human confirms some review slips, promote just those confirmed lines into the main graph and re-cluster. Either way the rule is the same: confident lines build entities; shaky lines build a review worklist.
Analogy — from residents to households
Back to the census. Pass one grouped forms into residents — that's done; every resident now wears a stable badge from Stage 07. But the census also wants to know households: which residents live under one roof. A household is not one person — it's several distinct people who share an address and a last name. So you run the grouping again, except now each resident (with their badge) is a single dot, and you draw a line between two residents when they share a roof, not when they're the same human. Same circle-finding machinery, brand-new meaning for "dot" and "line."

Household clustering — same algorithm, new dots, new lines

This is the key mental move of the stage, so let's make the swap explicit. Compare the two passes side by side:

Pass 1 — finding people (Stages 01–07)Pass 2 — finding households (now)
A dot is…one recordone resolved person (an entity_id)
A line means…"same person" (a match verdict)"same household" (shared address + last name)
A circle is…a master entity (one human)a household (several humans, one roof)
Algorithmconnected componentsconnected components — identical

Because the algorithm is literally the same connectedComponents(), you don't learn anything new about how — you only change what you feed it. The dots are now your entities; the lines come from a self-join that pairs entities sharing a normalized address and surname. This is what "clustering on top of clustering" means, and why it's called secondary clustering — it runs on the output of the first clustering, not on raw records.

household clustering — entities as dots, "shared roof" as lines
from pyspark.sql import functions as F # entities = one row per resolved person: (entity_id, last_name, address_norm) # JOB: draw a "same household" line between two DIFFERENT entities that # share a normalized address AND a last name left = entities.alias("l") right = entities.alias("r") household_edges = ( left.join(right, (F.col("l.address_norm") == F.col("r.address_norm")) & (F.col("l.last_name") == F.col("r.last_name")) & (F.col("l.entity_id") < F.col("r.entity_id"))) # < avoids self/dup pairs .select(F.col("l.entity_id").alias("src"), F.col("r.entity_id").alias("dst")) ) # SAME machinery as Stage 06 — only the dots and lines changed g2 = GraphFrame(entities.select(F.col("entity_id").alias("id")), household_edges) households = g2.connectedComponents() # gives each entity a household id
The mistake that defines this stage — never collapse a household into a person. A household clusters distinct people who share a roof; it is emphatically not a signal to merge them into one golden record. Jon, his wife, and his teenage kid share 200 Pine Ave and the surname Smith — three entities, one household, three separate humans with three separate SSNs. Merge them and you've fused a family into a person: a classic, embarrassing MDM data-quality bug. Households link entities (entity-to-entity relationships); they never dissolve them.

Cluster aggregation — rolling people up into the household record

Once each entity carries a household id, the last move is to aggregate: group the entities by household to produce a household-level row — size, the shared address, the list of member entity_ids, maybe a designated head-of-household. This is the same group-and-summarize you already know, just one level up the ladder (records → entities → households):

The same pattern generalizes to any "entity-to-entity" link, not just households. Swap the line rule and you get a different relationship graph over the very same entity dots: "same employer," "same insurance policy," "shares a bank account," "corporate parent ↔ subsidiary." Secondary clustering is the reusable engine — resolve individuals once, then keep re-clustering those resolved entities under whatever relationship you need. You now have the full back half of the pipeline: slips → people → stable names → households and beyond.

Honest Gaps
What this module didn't cover
Weighted / probabilistic clustering: we treated every match line as equally binding. Real systems sometimes weight lines by match confidence and use community-detection (edge-weighted label propagation, graph cuts) so a weak line is less able to fuse a crowd — a softer answer to Stage 05's bridge-edge risk than a hard post-hoc size check.

Entity-ID stability in depth: we framed hash-the-set vs persist-a-map and the overlap-reconciliation idea, but didn't build the full survivorship logic (what happens when one entity splits into two across runs, or how to keep an audit trail of ID merges/retirements over time).

Cluster-splitting / review tooling: we flag oversized clusters and route review-band groups to a queue, but didn't build the operational "split this cluster" workflow at the cluster level.

GraphFrames internals: connectedComponents() stayed a black box. Its actual distributed label-propagation algorithm and convergence/iteration behavior at scale weren't traced.

Graph algorithms beyond connectivity: centrality, shortest-path, fraud-ring community detection — useful on the same entity graph, but out of scope for straightforward resolution and household linking.

Module 06 · MDM / Spark Track · Phase 3 — ER at Scale

LSH & MinHash

Module 05 gave you the ThresholdTuner to optimize your field similarity weights and matching thresholds. But that tuner assumes you already have a set of candidate pairs. In a dataset with millions of customer records, how do you find those candidate pairs in the first place? Comparing every record to every other record is impossible at scale. This module introduces **Locality Sensitive Hashing (LSH)** and **MinHash** — the algorithms that bypass the quadratic bottleneck to generate fuzzy candidate pairs in $O(N)$ time.

What this module adds to your pipeline
Standardize→ Block (LSH)→ Self-Join→ Field Scoring→ Combine & Classify→ Tune & Calibrate→ Cluster

In Module 00 you saw that standard exact blocking key joins (e.g. comparing records only if they have the exact same `zip_code` or `soundex(last_name)`) are fast but brittle: a single typo splits the keys, and the records are never compared. MinHash and LSH solve this by projecting your records into hash buckets where similar records collide with high probability, even if they have typos across multiple fields.

The exact scaling challenge

You have a database of 1 million customers. If you run a naive nested loop to find matches, you must compare record 1 to record 2, record 1 to record 3, all the way to $N \times (N - 1) / 2$. For 1 million records, that requires **500 billion comparisons**. If each comparison takes 1 microsecond, your pipeline will run for **5.7 days**. If you scale to 10 million records, it will take **1.5 years**. Locality Sensitive Hashing cuts this search space down by 99.9% in minutes.

Concept 01
Jaccard Similarity
The mathematical ground truth for token similarity. It is the size of the intersection of two token sets divided by the size of their union. Essential for measuring shingled strings.
Concept 02
MinHash Signatures
A compression mechanism. Maps a large shingle set to a small vector of $K$ integers, such that the probability of signature elements matching is exactly their Jaccard similarity.
Concept 03
Banding (LSH)
Slicing signatures into $b$ bands of $r$ rows. Records are placed in buckets per band. Colliding in *at least one* band makes them a candidate pair, bypassing all-pairs comparison.
Concept 04
S-Curve Tuning
The non-linear probability curve $P = 1 - (1 - s^r)^b$. By adjusting $b$ and $r$, you tune a mathematical threshold that controls your candidate generator's precision and recall.

Stage 01

The N² Scaling Wall & Fuzzy Blocking

To perform entity resolution, you must compare records. But comparing everything to everything creates a quadratic explosion ($N^2$) that crashes cluster memory. Exact blocking stops this explosion, but at a severe cost to match recall.

The quadratic explosion

The math is simple and unforgiving. As the number of records $N$ grows, the number of candidate pairs grows quadratically:

Exact Blocking: A rigid compromise

To survive, pipelines use **Blocking Keys** (e.g. comparing records only if they have the exact same `zip_code` or `soundex(last_name)`). While this reduces the comparisons from $O(N^2)$ to local partitions, it introduces a hard boundary:

✗ Rigid Blocking Key Failure
"We only compare if ZIP codes match exactly."

Record A: Jon Smith, ZIP 90210
Record B: Jon Smith, ZIP 90211 (typist slipped)

Because the ZIP codes differ, these records land in separate partitions. They will **never** be compared, resulting in a permanent False Negative.

✓ Fuzzy Blocking (LSH) Solution
"We compare if they share enough fuzzy features."

Instead of relying on a single field matching exactly, LSH shingles the records and groups them if their *overall token similarity* is high.

The typo in the ZIP code is outweighed by the similarity in name and address, allowing the pair to collide and merge.

The blocking key trap: Adding more blocking keys (e.g., zip, phone, email) increases your chances of finding matches, but running multiple exact join queries creates massive shuffle overhead. We need an algorithm that hashes records based on similarity directly — Locality Sensitive Hashing.

The Goal of LSH

LSH is a **hashing family** where:

  • If two records are **highly similar**, their hash values are **highly likely to collide**.
  • If two records are **different**, their hash values are **highly unlikely to collide**.
By placing records into hash buckets, we restrict our pairwise comparisons *only* to records that share a bucket.

Stage 02

Jaccard Similarity — Token Sets

To measure similarity between two records, we convert them into sets of tokens (shingles). Jaccard similarity is the overlap metric that forms the foundation of MinHash and LSH.

String Shingling (k-grams)

A shingle is a contiguous substring of length $k$ (character level) or a token of length $n$ (word level). Character shingling turns a raw string into a set, absorbing minor typos.

Let's use 2-shingles ($k=2$) on name fields (case-normalized, whitespace collapsed):

"JON" → {'JO', 'ON'}
"JOHN" → {'JO', 'OH', 'HN'}

The Jaccard Formula

The Jaccard similarity $J(A, B)$ of two sets $A$ and $B$ is the size of their intersection divided by the size of their union:

J(A, B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|
Analogy — The Venn Diagram overlay
Imagine you have two blueprints. If you slide one blueprint over the other, the intersection is the area where the drawings line up exactly. The union is the total area covered by both blueprints combined.

Jaccard similarity is the percentage of the total area that overlaps. If the blueprints are identical, overlap is 100% (1.0). If they share absolutely nothing, overlap is 0% (0.0). A single typo is just a small line that doesn't overlap, keeping the overall percentage high.

Why shingle size matters: If $k$ is too small (e.g. 1-shingles, plain characters), strings with completely different meanings share high overlap (e.g., "MARIA" and "AMIRA" share 100% of characters). If $k$ is too large (e.g. 5-shingles), a single typo ruins multiple shingles, making Jaccard fall to 0. For name/address data, **2-grams or 3-grams** are the optimal sweet spot.

Stage 03

The MinHash Trick — Signature Matrices

To compute Jaccard similarity, you must compare shingle sets directly. But distributing sets across a cluster is slow and memory-intensive. MinHashing compresses shingle sets into fixed-size integer signatures that preserve Jaccard similarity.

The MinHash Core Property

If we apply a random permutation (shuffle) to the rows of our shingles universe, the index of the first row containing a 1 for a record is its **MinHash**.

The mathematical magic of MinHash is that:

P( MinHash(A) == MinHash(B) ) = Jaccard(A, B)
By generating $K$ different random permutations, we get $K$ MinHash values for each record. This vector of $K$ values is the record's **MinHash Signature**. The fraction of matching elements between two signatures is an unbiased estimator of their Jaccard similarity.

Worked Example — The Permutation Matrix

Let's take three shingle sets: Record A: {JO, ON}, Record B: {JO, ON, OH}, Record C: {RI, IA}. We apply 3 random hash functions (representing permutations of the shingles universe):

Comparing Signatures vs Comparing Sets

Instead of comparing large, variable-sized sets across the cluster, we now compare fixed-size arrays (e.g. $K=100$ integers).

  • Signature similarity $Sim(A, B) = \frac{\text{matching slots}}{K}$
  • For A and B: matches at index 1, 2, 3 → $3 / 3 = 1.0$ (approximating Jaccard 0.67)
  • For A and C: matches at index 1 → $0 / 3 = 0.0$ (approximating Jaccard 0.0)
As $K$ increases, the estimated signature similarity converges to the true Jaccard similarity.

Stage 04

Banding & Locality Sensitive Hashing

MinHash signatures compress your data, but comparing all signature pairs is still $O(N^2)$. Banding is the magic step that groups similar signatures into hash buckets without performing a single pairwise comparison.

The Banding Mechanism

Take your MinHash signature of length $K$ (e.g., $K = 100$ integers). Slice it into $b$ non-overlapping bands, where each band contains exactly $r$ rows. Thus, $K = b \times r$.

Signature Vector (Length 9)Band 1 ($r=3$)Band 2 ($r=3$)Band 3 ($r=3$)
[12, 5, 87, 3, 14, 9, 88, 41, 2][12, 5, 87][3, 14, 9][88, 41, 2]

For each band, we pass the $r$ integers into a standard hash function (e.g., MurmurHash) that maps the vector to a bucket ID.

  • If two records share the **exact same signature elements** in a band, they hash to the same bucket ID for that band.
  • If they collide in **at least one** of the $b$ bands, they are nominated as a **candidate pair**.

Analogy — sorting mail into bags
Imagine you have thousands of books. You want to find books with similar chapters. Comparing every page of every book is too slow.

Instead, you divide each book's summary into 5 sections (bands). You write the words of section 1 on a card, put it in an envelope, and drop it in slot 1. You do the same for sections 2 through 5. If two books share the exact same envelope contents in slot 3, they land in the same bag.

You only compare books that land in the same bag. Even if a book has a typo in section 1, it will still collide in sections 2, 3, 4, or 5, ensuring you don't miss it.

The Red Herring: "LSH is a replacement for scoring"

✗ Red Herring — standard mistake
"We will use LSH bucket collisions to declare matches."

LSH is an approximate algorithm. It has false positives: unrelated records that happen to collide in one band (e.g. sharing a common name prefix but differing elsewhere).

Using LSH alone to merge records will corrupt your golden entities.

✓ What actually decides
LSH is a candidate generator (blocking mechanism) only.

LSH filters the $N^2$ search space down to a thin stream of candidate pairs. You then pass this stream to your **ScoringPlugin** (Module 04) to calculate exact field weights and composite confidence.

LSH saves the cluster; ScoringPlugin saves the data quality.

Stage 05

Tuning LSH — The S-Curve Math

The probability of two records colliding in LSH is a non-linear function of their Jaccard similarity. By adjusting $b$ (bands) and $r$ (rows), you shape this curve to match your target similarity threshold.

The Collision Probability Formula

Let $s$ be the true Jaccard similarity between two records.

  1. Probability that signatures match in all rows of a single band: $s^r$
  2. Probability that signatures do *not* match in a single band: $1 - s^r$
  3. Probability that signatures do *not* match in any of the $b$ bands: $(1 - s^r)^b$
  4. Probability that signatures match in **at least one** band (collision probability):

P(collision) = 1 - (1 - s^r)^b

This formula plots an **S-Curve**. The curve is flat at low similarity, rises steeply near the threshold, and flattens out near 1.0.

The S-Curve Threshold (Elbow)

The "elbow" or transition threshold $t$ where collision probability is exactly 0.50 is approximated by:

t ≈ (1 / b)^(1 / r)

Worked Table — How $b$ and $r$ shape the curve

Let's compare two configurations for a signature size $K = 100$:

Jaccard $s$Config A: $b=20, r=5$ ($t \approx 0.55$)Config B: $b=5, r=20$ ($t \approx 0.92$)What it means
0.20$1 - (1 - 0.20^5)^{20} \approx \mathbf{0.006}$$1 - (1 - 0.20^{20})^5 \approx \mathbf{0.000}$Low similarities are safely filtered in both configs.

The trade-off:
  • **Too many bands $b$ (low $r$):** The threshold shifts left. You catch minor, typo-heavy matches (high recall), but you also generate massive amounts of junk candidate pairs (low precision, high cluster load).
  • **Too few bands $b$ (high $r$):** The threshold shifts right. Your candidate generation is very clean (high precision), but you will miss pairs with moderate typos (low recall).

Stage 06

MinHashLSH in PySpark

PySpark includes a built-in `MinHashLSH` algorithm inside the `pyspark.ml` library. Here is the operational code pattern to shingle strings, generate signatures, and run the similarity join.

The Vector Requirement

PySpark's LSH model expects inputs to be of type Vector (representing shingle counts/presence). We use RegexTokenizer to split strings, NGram to build character shingles, and CountVectorizer to convert shingle sets into vectors.

PySpark MinHashLSH candidate generation
from pyspark.ml.feature import RegexTokenizer, NGram, CountVectorizer, MinHashLSH from pyspark.sql import functions as F # 1. Shingle the standardized name column (character-level bigrams) # E.g., "jon smith" -> tokens -> ngrams -> vector tokenizer = RegexTokenizer(inputCol="name_std", outputCol="chars", pattern="") ngram = NGram(n=2, inputCol="chars", outputCol="shingles") vectorizer = CountVectorizer(inputCol="shingles", outputCol="features") # Fit vectorizer pipeline df_chars = tokenizer.transform(df) df_shingles = ngram.transform(df_chars) vector_model = vectorizer.fit(df_shingles) df_vectors = vector_model.transform(df_shingles) # 2. Fit MinHash LSH model (K = 60 hash tables) lsh = MinHashLSH(inputCol="features", outputCol="hashes", numHashTables=60) lsh_model = lsh.fit(df_vectors) # 3. Perform approximate similarity join # threshold = 1 - target_jaccard (0.40 distance means >= 0.60 similarity) candidates_df = lsh_model.approxSimilarityJoin( df_vectors, df_vectors, threshold=0.40, distCol="jaccard_dist" ).filter("datasetA.id < datasetB.id") # remove duplicates/self-comparisons candidates_df.select( F.col("datasetA.id").alias("id_a"), F.col("datasetB.id").alias("id_b"), (1.0 - F.col("jaccard_dist")).alias("jaccard_sim") ).show()
The PySpark LSH Distance Trap: PySpark's LSH join uses Jaccard Distance ($D = 1 - S$), not similarity. If you want to find candidate pairs with Jaccard similarity $\ge 0.70$, you must set threshold=0.30. If you pass threshold=0.70, you are asking for similarity $\ge 0.30$, which will flood your cluster with false positives.

Stage 07

The Hybrid Pipeline — Merging Candidates

In a production Master Data Management (MDM) system, LSH candidate generation does not run in isolation. It operates alongside traditional exact blocking channels to form a hybrid candidate stream.

The Candidate Flow

Traditional blocking (e.g. matching on exact `zip_code` or `soundex(last_name)`) is great for catching records that share specific field values. LSH is great for catching typo-heavy records across multiple fields. By combining both, you maximize recall without exploding cluster memory.

hybrid pipeline candidate generation
1. Generate Exact Blocking Candidates
blocking_pairs = df.join(df, "zip_code").filter("a.id < b.id").select("id_a", "id_b")
2. Generate LSH Candidates
lsh_pairs = lsh_model.approxSimilarityJoin(..., threshold=0.4).select("id_a", "id_b")
3. Combine and Deduplicate (Union & Distinct)
all_candidates = blocking_pairs.union(lsh_pairs).distinct()
The distinct() step removes pairs captured by both channels, ensuring you only score each unique pair once.

The complete architectural flow

hybrid pipeline join pattern
# 1. Exact blocking stream exact_candidates = df.alias("A").join( df.alias("B"), (F.col("A.zip_code") == F.col("B.zip_code")) & (F.col("A.id") < F.col("B.id")) ).select( F.col("A.id").alias("id_a"), F.col("B.id").alias("id_b") ) # 2. LSH fuzzy blocking stream lsh_candidates = lsh_model.approxSimilarityJoin( df_vectors, df_vectors, threshold=0.35 ).filter("datasetA.id < datasetB.id").select( F.col("datasetA.id").alias("id_a"), F.col("datasetB.id").alias("id_b") ) # 3. Deduplicated merge unified_candidates = exact_candidates.union(lsh_candidates).distinct() # 4. Feed unified candidates to ScoringPlugin (Module 04) scored_pairs = scoring_plugin.run(unified_candidates)
Why this works: LSH provides a safety net. If a record has a typo in the zip code, the exact blocking stream misses it, but LSH catches it. If a record has typos in the name, LSH might miss it, but the exact zip code blocking stream catches it. Together, they achieve near-perfect candidate recall.

Honest Gaps
What this module didn't cover
SimHash: An alternative LSH algorithm that estimates Cosine similarity rather than Jaccard similarity. Extremely useful for dense vector embeddings, but MinHash remains standard for token/shingle overlap.

Custom Hash Family Construction: The mathematical proof showing that minhash hash functions are indeed min-wise independent. We treat hashes as black-box generators in production PySpark.

Signature Join Partition Skew: Large clusters of records matching on empty/default values (e.g. null name fields) can create LSH signature hot spots, causing cluster executors to out-of-memory. Solved by pre-filtering default values.

Multi-Probe LSH: Querying adjacent buckets to find near-collisions when signatures land close to band boundaries. PySpark LSH does not support multi-probing out of the box.

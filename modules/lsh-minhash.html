LSH & MinHash

Module 06 gave you the ThresholdTuner to optimize your field similarity weights and matching thresholds. But that tuner assumes you already have a set of candidate pairs. In a dataset with millions of customer records, how do you find those candidate pairs in the first place? Comparing every record to every other record is impossible at scale. This module introduces **Locality Sensitive Hashing (LSH)** and **MinHash** — the algorithms that bypass the quadratic bottleneck to generate fuzzy candidate pairs in $O(N)$ time.

What this module adds to your pipeline
Standardize→ Block (LSH)→ Self-Join→ Field Scoring→ Combine & Classify→ Tune & Calibrate→ Cluster

In Module 00 you saw that standard exact blocking key joins (e.g. comparing records only if they have the exact same `zip_code` or `soundex(last_name)`) are fast but brittle: a single typo splits the keys, and the records are never compared. MinHash and LSH solve this by projecting your records into hash buckets where similar records collide with high probability, even if they have typos across multiple fields.

The one question this whole module answers

You have a million customer records and you can't afford to compare them all to each other. So here's the single question every stage below is chasing:

How do you find just the handful of record pairs that are actually similar — without ever looking at most pairs at all?

That "without ever looking at most pairs" is the hard part. A naive nested loop compares record 1 to every other record, then record 2 to every other, all the way to $N \times (N-1)/2$ pairs. For 1 million records that's **500 billion comparisons** — at 1 microsecond each, your pipeline runs for **5.7 days**; at 10 million records, **1.5 years**. We need a trick that surfaces the similar pairs directly, never paying for the rest. The chain below builds that trick one link at a time — each link exists only because the previous one left a gap.

Link 01 · Jaccard
"How similar are two records?"
First we need a number for similarity at all. Jaccard answers "of all the pieces these two records have between them, what fraction do they share?" Everything downstream is built to estimate this one number cheaply.
Link 02 · MinHash
"Can we estimate it without the full record?"
Comparing whole records is expensive. MinHash shrinks each record to a short fingerprint of $K$ numbers — built so that the fraction of fingerprint slots two records share is their Jaccard similarity.
Link 03 · Banding
"Can we avoid comparing fingerprints too?"
Even comparing every pair of fingerprints is still too many. Banding sorts records into bins by chunks of their fingerprint, so we only ever compare records that already landed in the same bin.
Link 04 · The S-curve
"How picky should the bins be?"
Bin size is the one knob you turn. Make it stricter and you catch only near-identical records; looser and you catch typo-heavy ones too — at the cost of junk. The S-curve is the picture of that trade-off.
How to read this module: it's a single chain, not a menu. Each stage ends by exposing a gap that the next stage exists to close — Jaccard gives us the target number, but it's too slow to compute → MinHash estimates it cheaply, but comparing fingerprints is still $O(N^2)$ → banding removes that too, but introduces one knob → the S-curve shows how to set the knob. Read it top to bottom and you should never have to ask "why are we doing this next?" — the previous stage will have told you.

Stage 01

The N² Scaling Wall & Fuzzy Blocking

To perform entity resolution, you must compare records. But comparing everything to everything creates a quadratic explosion ($N^2$) that crashes cluster memory. Exact blocking stops this explosion, but at a severe cost to match recall.

Why you can't just compare everything

The math is simple and unforgiving. As the number of records $N$ grows, the number of candidate pairs grows quadratically:

1,000,000499,999,500,0005.8 daysCluster crashes or stalls
10,000,00049,999,995,000,0001.58 yearsProduction failure

The usual escape hatch — and where it leaks

To survive, pipelines use **Blocking Keys** (e.g. comparing records only if they have the exact same `zip_code` or `soundex(last_name)`). While this reduces the comparisons from $O(N^2)$ to local partitions, it introduces a hard boundary:

✗ Rigid Blocking Key Failure
"We only compare if ZIP codes match exactly."

Record A: Jon Smith, ZIP 90210
Record B: Jon Smith, ZIP 90211 (typist slipped)

Because the ZIP codes differ, these records land in separate partitions. They will **never** be compared, resulting in a permanent False Negative.

✓ Fuzzy Blocking (LSH) Solution
"We compare if they share enough fuzzy features."

Instead of relying on a single field matching exactly, LSH shingles the records and groups them if their *overall token similarity* is high.

The typo in the ZIP code is outweighed by the similarity in name and address, allowing the pair to collide and merge.

The blocking key trap: Adding more blocking keys (e.g., zip, phone, email) increases your chances of finding matches, but running multiple exact join queries creates massive shuffle overhead. We need an algorithm that hashes records based on similarity directly — Locality Sensitive Hashing.

What we actually want: hashing by similarity

So exact blocking is too rigid (one typo and the pair is lost forever) and all-pairs is too slow. What we want sits between them — a hashing family where:

  • If two records are **highly similar**, their hash values are **highly likely to collide**.
  • If two records are **different**, their hash values are **highly unlikely to collide**.
Place records into buckets with that property, and you only ever compare records that share a bucket. That's Locality Sensitive Hashing.

The gap this leaves open → "Highly similar" is hand-wavy. Before we can build a hash that fires on similarity, we have to define similarity as a number — one we can point at and say "these two are 0.7 alike." That number is Jaccard similarity, and it's the next link in the chain.

Stage 02

Jaccard Similarity — Token Sets

We need a number for "how similar are two records." It comes from one simple idea: chop each record into little pieces, then ask what fraction of pieces the two records share. That fraction is Jaccard similarity — the target number the rest of the module spends its energy estimating cheaply.

The picture to hold for the whole module — two kids and their stickers
Give each record a kid. The kid's sticker collection is the set of little pieces that record breaks into. Two records are "similar" when their two kids own a lot of the same stickers.

Jaccard is just: out of every distinct sticker the two kids own between them, what fraction do they both have? Identical collections → 1.0. Nothing in common → 0.0. One typo only swaps out a sticker or two, so the fraction stays high — which is exactly why this survives messy data. Keep these two kids in mind; every later stage is just a faster way to estimate this one fraction.

What the "stickers" actually are (shingles)

The pieces are called shingles: a shingle is a contiguous substring of length $k$ (character level) or a token of length $n$ (word level). Character shingling turns a raw string into a set of stickers, absorbing minor typos.

Let's use 2-shingles ($k=2$) on name fields (case-normalized, whitespace collapsed):

"JON" → stickers {'JO', 'ON'}
"JOHN" → stickers {'JO', 'OH', 'HN'}

The fraction, written as a formula

Now that the picture is in place, the formula is just it in symbols. Calling the two sticker collections $A$ and $B$, Jaccard similarity is the shared stickers ($A \cap B$) over all distinct stickers ($A \cup B$):

J(A, B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B| · "shared stickers ÷ all stickers"

Why shingle size matters: If $k$ is too small (e.g. 1-shingles, plain characters), strings with completely different meanings share high overlap (e.g., "MARIA" and "AMIRA" share 100% of characters). If $k$ is too large (e.g. 5-shingles), a single typo ruins multiple shingles, making Jaccard fall to 0. For name/address data, **2-grams or 3-grams** are the optimal sweet spot.
The gap this leaves open → We have our target number. But look at what computing it costs: to get the shared fraction you must hold both kids' full sticker collections and intersect them — and to find matches you'd do that for every pair of records. That's the same all-pairs bill from Stage 01, just dressed up. The next question is the whole trick: can we estimate that same fraction from a tiny fingerprint, without ever comparing full collections? That's MinHash.

Stage 03

The MinHash Trick — From Sets to Fingerprints

This is the sleight of hand the whole module is built on. We're going to estimate the shared-sticker fraction from a short fingerprint — and we'll never once compare two full sticker collections to do it. The mechanism looks like a game.

The bag trick

Same two kids — now play a game with one bag
Take every distinct sticker the two kids own between them and throw them all into one bag. Shuffle it. Now pull stickers out one at a time, and each kid watches for the first sticker pulled that they personally own — that's their answer for this round.

Here's the only rule that matters: the two kids give the same answer exactly when the first sticker pulled out of the bag — the very first one either of them owns — happens to be one they both own. If the first one out is owned by only one kid, their answers differ that round.

Play the round once and you get a yes/no. Play it many times (re-shuffle each round) and the fraction of rounds they agree creeps toward something meaningful — and you never compared their collections directly. You only ever asked each kid one thing per round: "what's your first?"

Watch it happen — and watch the number emerge

Let Ana own stickers {A, B, C, D} and Ben own {A, B, C, E}. Between them the bag holds 5 distinct stickers {A, B, C, D, E}; they share 3 of them {A, B, C}. So their true Jaccard is $3/5 = \mathbf{0.6}$ — that's the number we're secretly trying to recover, without computing it directly. Five shuffles:

RoundOrder pulled from bagAna's first ownedBen's first ownedAgree?
1D, A, E, B, CDA✗ (first out, D, is Ana-only)
2C, E, A, B, DCC✓ (first out, C, is shared)
3E, D, A, B, CDE✗ (first out, E, is Ben-only)
4B, A, C, E, DBB✓ (first out, B, is shared)
5A, C, E, B, DAA✓ (first out, A, is shared)
Agreement rate over 5 rounds3 / 5 = 0.60

Three agreements out of five — 0.60, landing right on the true Jaccard. That's not luck. Look at column "Agree?": the round agrees precisely when the first sticker out of the bag is one of the shared ones. Since the shuffle is fair, the first sticker out is equally likely to be any of the 5 in the bag — so the chance it's a shared one is exactly $3/5$. The agreement rate is the shared fraction. Written as the one line you'll see in textbooks:

P( both kids give the same answer ) = |shared| / |all| = Jaccard(A, B)

Five rounds is noisy (it could have come out 2/5 or 4/5 on a different day). Play $K$ rounds — say $K=100$ — and the agreement rate tightens around the true 0.6. That's the entire idea: turn an expensive set-overlap into a cheap coin we can flip as many times as we like.

From rounds to a fingerprint (the signature)

In code we don't literally shuffle a bag — each "shuffle" is a hash function that orders the stickers, and each kid's answer for that round is the smallest hash value among the stickers they own (hence Min-Hash). Record each kid's $K$ answers in order and you get their MinHash signature: a fixed-length vector of $K$ numbers, the same length for everyone no matter how many stickers they had.

  • A 200-sticker record and a 3-sticker record both compress to, say, $K=100$ numbers.
  • To estimate two records' Jaccard, you don't touch their stickers again — you just count how many of the $K$ signature slots match, position by position, and divide by $K$.
  • Bigger $K$ → more rounds → tighter estimate. That's the only knob here, and it just trades compute for accuracy.
The gap this leaves open → We've shrunk every record to $K$ numbers, and we can estimate similarity by comparing two fingerprints slot-by-slot. Huge win — but notice what we haven't fixed: to actually find the matches we'd still compare every fingerprint against every other fingerprint. That's $N^2$ all over again, just cheaper per comparison. The last link asks the boldest question yet: can we avoid comparing fingerprints to each other at all? That's banding.

Stage 04

Banding & Locality Sensitive Hashing

MinHash signatures compress your data, but comparing all signature pairs is still $O(N^2)$. Banding is the magic step that groups similar signatures into hash buckets without performing a single pairwise comparison.

The idea: sort kids into bins, then only look inside a bin

Still the same kids — now we stop comparing fingerprints one-by-one
Each kid now carries a fingerprint: their $K$ round-answers in a row. Chop that fingerprint into a few equal chunks — say a 100-number fingerprint into 20 chunks of 5 numbers each.

Now run a mailroom. For each chunk position, every kid drops into the bin labelled by the exact contents of that chunk. Two kids land in the same bin for a chunk only if that whole chunk of their fingerprint is identical. Then the rule: any two kids who ever share a bin become a candidate pair — and those are the only pairs you ever actually compare.

Why this is safe: a real near-duplicate agrees on most rounds, so at least one of its chunks will match exactly and land it in a shared bin. A single typo only disturbs a round or two — it can't ruin every chunk. So you skip almost all pairs, yet rarely drop a true match. You never compared two fingerprints directly; the bins did the work.

The same thing in symbols

"Fingerprint of $K$ numbers, chopped into chunks" written formally: take the MinHash signature of length $K$ and slice it into $b$ non-overlapping bands ("chunks"), each holding exactly $r$ rows ("numbers per chunk"). So $K = b \times r$. Here $K=9$ sliced into $b=3$ bands of $r=3$:

Signature Vector (Length 9)Band 1 ($r=3$)Band 2 ($r=3$)Band 3 ($r=3$)
[12, 5, 87, 3, 14, 9, 88, 41, 2][12, 5, 87][3, 14, 9][88, 41, 2]

The "bin" is just a hash bucket: for each band we feed its $r$ integers into a standard hash function (e.g. MurmurHash) to get a bucket ID. That makes the mailroom concrete —

  • Two records with the **exact same signature elements** in a band hash to the **same bucket ID** for that band (same bin).
  • Collide in **at least one** of the $b$ bands → nominated as a **candidate pair**.

One warning before we tune it

The banding mailroom is now a complete candidate generator — but it's tempting to over-trust it. Before we turn the one knob it exposes, settle what banding is and isn't for:

✗ Red Herring — standard mistake
"We will use LSH bucket collisions to declare matches."

LSH is an approximate algorithm. It has false positives: unrelated records that happen to collide in one band (e.g. sharing a common name prefix but differing elsewhere).

Using LSH alone to merge records will corrupt your golden entities.

✓ What actually decides
LSH is a candidate generator (blocking mechanism) only.

LSH filters the $N^2$ search space down to a thin stream of candidate pairs. You then pass this stream to your **ScoringPlugin** (Module 04) to calculate exact field weights and composite confidence.

LSH saves the cluster; ScoringPlugin saves the data quality.

The gap this leaves open → Banding works, but it handed us a knob and walked off: how big should each chunk be, and how many chunks? Make chunks big and a pair must agree on a long run of rounds to ever share a bin — very picky, misses typo-heavy matches. Make them small and near-strangers start colliding — junk floods in. The last stage is the dial for exactly this: how picky the bins are, and the S-shaped curve that picture makes.

Stage 05

Tuning LSH — The S-Curve Math

The knob from the last stage is really one question: how picky are the bins? Pick the chunk size and chunk count, and you've decided which pairs collide. Let's feel that out on real numbers first — the famous "S-curve" is just the shape those numbers trace, not a formula to memorize.

Trace one pair through the bins

Take a config of $b = 20$ chunks ("bands") of $r = 5$ rounds each ("rows"), and ask: a pair that's truly $s = 0.7$ alike — what's the chance they share a bin? Walk it, one step at a time:

does this pair land in a shared bin? (s = 0.70, r = 5, b = 20)
1 · For ONE chunk to match, all 5 of its rounds must agree.
each round agrees with prob s = 0.7 → whole chunk matches with prob 0.7⁵ ≈ 0.168 (~1 in 6)
A single chunk matching is unlikely — that feels like bad news for catching this pair.
2 · But you only need ONE of the 20 chunks to match.
chance a given chunk does NOT match = 1 − 0.168 = 0.832 → chance ALL 20 fail = 0.832²⁰ ≈ 0.025
Twenty independent shots at a 1-in-6 event: almost certainly at least one lands.
3 · So they DO share a bin with the leftover probability.
P(share a bin) = 1 − 0.025 ≈ 0.975 → a 70%-similar pair is caught ~97% of the time

Now rerun the exact same walk for a near-stranger at $s = 0.4$: one chunk matches with prob $0.4^5 \approx 0.010$, all 20 fail with prob $0.990^{20} \approx 0.815$, so they share a bin only $\approx \mathbf{0.185}$ — about 18%. That jump — 18% at similarity 0.4, but 97% at 0.7 — is the whole point. The chance stays low for dissimilar pairs, then rises steeply through a narrow band of similarity, then saturates near 1. Plot it and you get an S:

The same walk, written as a formula

Every step you just traced is one line of algebra. Let $s$ be the true Jaccard similarity:

  1. One chunk's 5 rounds all agree: $s^r$  (step 1: $0.7^5$)
  2. That chunk does not fully match: $1 - s^r$
  3. None of the $b$ chunks match: $(1 - s^r)^b$  (step 2: $0.832^{20}$)
  4. At least one chunk matches → they share a bin:
P(collision) = 1 - (1 - s^r)^b

That's it — the formula is just the trace you already did, with $0.7$ replaced by $s$. Flat at low similarity, steep through the middle, flat near 1.0: the S-curve.

Where you set the dial: the threshold

The one number you actually care about is where the curve makes its steep jump — the similarity at which a pair becomes more-likely-than-not to share a bin. That "elbow" threshold $t$ (where collision probability crosses 0.50) is set entirely by your choice of $b$ and $r$:

t ≈ (1 / b)^(1 / r)

So tuning LSH is really just: pick the similarity $t$ you want to catch, then choose $b$ and $r$ that put the elbow there. Watch two configs move the elbow:

Watch the curve move with $b$ and $r$

Same fingerprint length $K = 100$, two very different dials:

The trade-off:
  • **Too many bands $b$ (low $r$):** The threshold shifts left. You catch minor, typo-heavy matches (high recall), but you also generate massive amounts of junk candidate pairs (low precision, high cluster load).
  • **Too few bands $b$ (high $r$):** The threshold shifts right. Your candidate generation is very clean (high precision), but you will miss pairs with moderate typos (low recall).
The chain is complete → define similarity (Jaccard) → estimate it from a fingerprint (MinHash) → skip the fingerprint comparisons with bins (banding) → set how picky the bins are (the S-curve). Every link closed the gap the one before it left open. From here it's no longer "why" — it's "how, in code." Stage 06

MinHashLSH in PySpark

PySpark includes a built-in `MinHashLSH` algorithm inside the `pyspark.ml` library. Here is the operational code pattern to shingle strings, generate signatures, and run the similarity join.

The Vector Requirement

PySpark's LSH model expects inputs to be of type Vector (representing shingle counts/presence). We use RegexTokenizer to split strings, NGram to build character shingles, and CountVectorizer to convert shingle sets into vectors.

PySpark MinHashLSH candidate generation
from pyspark.ml.feature import RegexTokenizer, NGram, CountVectorizer, MinHashLSH from pyspark.sql import functions as F # 1. Shingle the standardized name column (character-level bigrams) # E.g., "jon smith" -> tokens -> ngrams -> vector tokenizer = RegexTokenizer(inputCol="name_std", outputCol="chars", pattern="") ngram = NGram(n=2, inputCol="chars", outputCol="shingles") vectorizer = CountVectorizer(inputCol="shingles", outputCol="features") # Fit vectorizer pipeline df_chars = tokenizer.transform(df) df_shingles = ngram.transform(df_chars) vector_model = vectorizer.fit(df_shingles) df_vectors = vector_model.transform(df_shingles) # 2. Fit MinHash LSH model (K = 60 hash tables) lsh = MinHashLSH(inputCol="features", outputCol="hashes", numHashTables=60) lsh_model = lsh.fit(df_vectors) # 3. Perform approximate similarity join # threshold = 1 - target_jaccard (0.40 distance means >= 0.60 similarity) candidates_df = lsh_model.approxSimilarityJoin( df_vectors, df_vectors, threshold=0.40, distCol="jaccard_dist" ).filter("datasetA.id < datasetB.id") # remove duplicates/self-comparisons candidates_df.select( F.col("datasetA.id").alias("id_a"), F.col("datasetB.id").alias("id_b"), (1.0 - F.col("jaccard_dist")).alias("jaccard_sim") ).show()
The PySpark LSH Distance Trap: PySpark's LSH join uses Jaccard Distance ($D = 1 - S$), not similarity. If you want to find candidate pairs with Jaccard similarity $\ge 0.70$, you must set threshold=0.30. If you pass threshold=0.70, you are asking for similarity $\ge 0.30$, which will flood your cluster with false positives.

Stage 07

The Hybrid Pipeline — Merging Candidates

In a production Master Data Management (MDM) system, LSH candidate generation does not run in isolation. It operates alongside traditional exact blocking channels to form a hybrid candidate stream.

The Candidate Flow

Traditional blocking (e.g. matching on exact `zip_code` or `soundex(last_name)`) is great for catching records that share specific field values. LSH is great for catching typo-heavy records across multiple fields. By combining both, you maximize recall without exploding cluster memory.

hybrid pipeline candidate generation
1. Generate Exact Blocking Candidates
blocking_pairs = df.join(df, "zip_code").filter("a.id < b.id").select("id_a", "id_b")
2. Generate LSH Candidates
lsh_pairs = lsh_model.approxSimilarityJoin(..., threshold=0.4).select("id_a", "id_b")
3. Combine and Deduplicate (Union & Distinct)
all_candidates = blocking_pairs.union(lsh_pairs).distinct()
The distinct() step removes pairs captured by both channels, ensuring you only score each unique pair once.

The complete architectural flow

hybrid pipeline join pattern
# 1. Exact blocking stream exact_candidates = df.alias("A").join( df.alias("B"), (F.col("A.zip_code") == F.col("B.zip_code")) & (F.col("A.id") < F.col("B.id")) ).select( F.col("A.id").alias("id_a"), F.col("B.id").alias("id_b") ) # 2. LSH fuzzy blocking stream lsh_candidates = lsh_model.approxSimilarityJoin( df_vectors, df_vectors, threshold=0.35 ).filter("datasetA.id < datasetB.id").select( F.col("datasetA.id").alias("id_a"), F.col("datasetB.id").alias("id_b") ) # 3. Deduplicated merge unified_candidates = exact_candidates.union(lsh_candidates).distinct() # 4. Feed unified candidates to ScoringPlugin (Module 04) scored_pairs = scoring_plugin.run(unified_candidates)
Why this works: LSH provides a safety net. If a record has a typo in the zip code, the exact blocking stream misses it, but LSH catches it. If a record has typos in the name, LSH might miss it, but the exact zip code blocking stream catches it. Together, they achieve near-perfect candidate recall.

What this module didn't cover
SimHash: An alternative LSH algorithm that estimates Cosine similarity rather than Jaccard similarity. Extremely useful for dense vector embeddings, but MinHash remains standard for token/shingle overlap.

Custom Hash Family Construction: The mathematical proof showing that minhash hash functions are indeed min-wise independent. We treat hashes as black-box generators in production PySpark.

Signature Join Partition Skew: Large clusters of records matching on empty/default values (e.g. null name fields) can create LSH signature hot spots, causing cluster executors to out-of-memory. Solved by pre-filtering default values.

Multi-Probe LSH: Querying adjacent buckets to find near-collisions when signatures land close to band boundaries. PySpark LSH does not support multi-probing out of the box.

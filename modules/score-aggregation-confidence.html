Score Aggregation & Confidence

Module 04 gave you the ScoringPlugin — field weights, null renormalization, a composite score, and two thresholds that slice the number line into match / review / no-match. But those thresholds were guesses: 0.80 and 0.55, chosen by intuition. This module replaces intuition with evidence. You'll learn to measure how good your thresholds are, tune them against labeled data, and size the review queue so it actually fits your team's capacity.

What this module adds to your pipeline
Standardize→ Block→ Self-Join→ Field Scoring→ Combine & Classify→ Tune & Calibrate→ Cluster

In Module 04 you met the lever — precision vs recall — and saw that moving a threshold trades one for the other. But you didn't have the tools to measure either one, or to know where the best trade-off sits for your data. That's the gap this module fills.

The exact question, stated once

You have a working scorer. It produces a composite number for every candidate pair. You've picked two thresholds — match_threshold and review_threshold — that slice that number into verdicts. How do you know those thresholds are any good? How do you know you're not auto-merging too aggressively (corrupting records) or too conservatively (flooding your review queue with pairs that should have auto-merged)?

The answer is: you measure it. You get a set of pairs where a human has already said "yes, same person" or "no, different people." You run your scorer on those pairs, compare its verdicts to the human labels, and compute precision and recall. Then you slide the thresholds and watch how the numbers move. The threshold that gives you the best balance — for your business, not in the abstract — is the one you ship.

Concept 01
Labeled data
A sample of candidate pairs where a human has verified: true_match or true_non_match. This is your ground truth — the exam answer key your scorer is graded against.
Concept 02
Confusion matrix
A 2×2 table: true positives, false positives, false negatives, true negatives. Every scored pair lands in exactly one cell. This table is the raw material for precision and recall.
Concept 03
Precision & Recall
Precision = "of the pairs I merged, how many were real?" Recall = "of the real matches, how many did I catch?" Moving a threshold trades one for the other.
Concept 04
Confidence bands
Not every pair at score 0.65 is equally ambiguous. Calibrating the score to a probability — "65% of pairs scoring 0.65 are true matches" — lets you set thresholds with a meaning, not just a position.

Stage 01

Why Fixed Thresholds Fail

In Module 04 you set match_threshold = 0.80 and review_threshold = 0.55 by instinct. That's fine for a first run. But those numbers make an implicit promise about your data that you never checked — and on real data, the promise almost always breaks.

The implicit promise

When you set match_threshold = 0.80, you're implicitly saying: "every pair above 0.80 is a real match." When you set review_threshold = 0.55, you're saying: "no real match falls below 0.55." Both of those are empirical claims about the distribution of true matches in your data — and you made them without looking at the distribution.

Analogy — the exam grade boundary
Imagine you're setting the pass mark for an exam before you've seen a single student's paper. You pick 70% because it "feels right." But what if the exam was unusually hard and the best students scored in the 60s? Or unusually easy and everyone clears 80?

A pass mark only means something when you've seen the score distribution. In ER, the "exam" is your scorer and the "students" are candidate pairs. The threshold is the pass mark. Setting it before you see how real matches and non-matches distribute is the same mistake — and the consequences are merging wrong records or splitting right ones.

Two datasets, same thresholds, opposite disasters

The problem is concrete. Here are two real score distributions — same scorer, same thresholds, completely different outcomes:

Same thresholds. One dataset separates cleanly — the other doesn't. The problem isn't the scorer's logic (Module 04 got that right). The problem is that where you cut the number line needs to be fitted to the actual score distribution, not guessed.

The silent damage: a misplaced threshold doesn't error. It doesn't warn. It just silently splits real matches (missed merges → duplicates in your golden record) or merges false ones (corrupted entities → bad downstream analytics). You can run the pipeline for weeks before someone notices the golden record quality degrading — and by then you're hunting across the whole pipeline for the bug, when it was just a threshold that didn't fit the data.

What you need to replace guessing

Three things, in order:

Step 1
Labeled data
A sample of pairs where a human says ground truth — true_match or true_non_match. Without this, there is literally no way to measure whether your scorer is right or wrong.
Step 2
A measurement framework
Precision, recall, and the confusion matrix — the vocabulary for saying "this threshold catches 92% of true matches and wrongly merges 3% of non-matches."
Step 3
A tuning loop
Sweep the threshold across the score range, compute precision and recall at each point, and pick the threshold that gives your business the best trade-off — informed, not guessed.
The rest of this module builds these three pieces. Stage 02 covers labeled data. Stage 03 builds precision and recall from the confusion matrix. Stage 04 puts them on a curve. Stage 05 adds calibration. Stage 06 sizes the review queue. Stage 07 assembles the PySpark pattern.

Stage 02

Labeled Data — The Ground Truth You Need

Before you can measure anything about your scorer, you need an answer key. Labeled data is a sample of candidate pairs where a human has verified the truth: this pair is the same entity, or it isn't. Without it, you're optimizing in the dark.

What a labeled set looks like

Take a random sample of candidate pairs from your pipeline's output. For each pair, a human reviewer — someone who can look at the source records and decide — assigns a label:

How much labeled data do you need?

There's no magic number, but there are practical guardrails:

Sampling matters as much as size. If you only label pairs that scored above 0.70, you'll never know how many true matches your scorer placed below 0.70. A good labeled set is stratified: take some pairs from each score band (0–0.3, 0.3–0.5, 0.5–0.7, 0.7–0.9, 0.9–1.0) so you can see the full picture, not just the confident middle.

The red herring: "we'll label everything later"

✗ Red herring — what sounds responsible
"We'll label the full dataset when we have time."

The temptation is to delay tuning until you have a massive, perfectly labeled set. But that delays the only feedback loop that tells you whether your scorer is working.

Meanwhile, every pipeline run ships verdicts based on thresholds that are unvalidated.

✓ What actually decides
Label a small stratified sample now, tune, and iterate.

200 well-sampled pairs, labeled in a few hours, are enough to catch gross threshold misplacement — the kind that silently corrupts 20% of your merges.

Start small, tune, ship, label more later. A rough measurement beats no measurement every time.

Stage 03

Precision & Recall — Measuring Your Scorer

You have labels and you have verdicts. Now you compare them — systematically. Every scored pair falls into exactly one of four buckets in a confusion matrix. Precision and recall are two ratios you read straight off that matrix. Together they tell you exactly what your threshold is doing right and wrong.

The confusion matrix

Take your labeled set. Run each pair through the scorer. For each pair, compare what the scorer said (above match threshold → "predicted match") to what the human said (true_match / true_non_match). Each pair lands in one cell:

PREDICTED ↓
ACTUAL →
Actually a match
Actually NOT a match
Scorer says MATCH
TPTrue Positive — correct merge
FPFalse Positive — wrong merge
Scorer says NO MATCH
FNFalse Negative — missed match
TNTrue Negative — correct rejection

Every pair goes into exactly one cell. The counts in these four cells are the only raw data you need — everything else (precision, recall, F1) is derived from them.

Worked example — 200 labeled pairs

You labeled 200 pairs and ran the scorer at match_threshold = 0.80. Here's where they landed:

Actually NOT (120 total)

Precision: "of the ones I merged, how many were right?"

Precision reads across the top row — the pairs the scorer said "match" to:

precision calculation
precision = TP / (TP + FP)
precision = 68 / (68 + 4) = 68 / 72 = 0.944
94.4% of the pairs the scorer merged were genuine matches. The other 5.6% were wrong merges — two different people incorrectly combined into one entity.

Precision answers the business question: "how clean are my auto-merges?" High precision = few false merges = low record corruption. When a wrong merge is expensive (financial data, healthcare, legal), you want precision as high as possible.

Recall: "of the real matches, how many did I catch?"

Recall reads down the left column — the pairs that are actually matches:

recall calculation
recall = TP / (TP + FN)
recall = 68 / (68 + 12) = 68 / 80 = 0.850
85.0% of the real matches were caught by the scorer. The other 15% were missed — genuine same-person pairs that fell below the threshold and were rejected (or sent to review).

Recall answers the business question: "how many real matches am I missing?" High recall = few missed matches = few duplicate records surviving in the golden record. When duplicate records are expensive (marketing overcounting, fragmented customer view), you want recall as high as possible.

The trade-off, concretely

Raise match_threshold → 0.90
Precision ↑ Recall ↓

Fewer pairs clear the bar → fewer auto-merges → almost all of them are correct (precision rises). But some real matches that scored 0.80–0.89 now drop into review or rejection (recall falls).

You gain: cleaner golden record, fewer corrupt merges.
You lose: more unresolved duplicates, bigger review queue.

Lower match_threshold → 0.70
Recall ↑ Precision ↓

More pairs clear the bar → more auto-merges → you catch more real matches (recall rises). But some false matches that scored 0.70–0.79 slip through as wrong merges (precision falls).

You gain: fewer duplicates, more resolved entities.
You lose: more corrupt merges, dirtier golden record.

This is why you can't maximize both. Precision and recall pull in opposite directions as you move the threshold. The art is finding the point that balances the cost of false merges against the cost of missed matches — for your business, in your data. The PR curve in Stage 04 is the tool that shows you every possible balance at once.

Stage 04

The PR Curve — Every Threshold at Once

Instead of evaluating one threshold, sweep across every possible threshold from 0.0 to 1.0, compute precision and recall at each, and plot them. The result is a precision-recall curve — a single picture that shows you every trade-off your scorer can make, so you can pick the right one with evidence.

How to build it

The algorithm is mechanical. For each threshold t from 0.0 to 1.0 in small steps:

build a PR curve from labeled data
def pr_curve(labeled_pairs, thresholds): """labeled_pairs: list of (composite_score, is_true_match)""" points = [] for t in thresholds: tp = sum(1 for score, label in labeled_pairs if score >= t and label == True) fp = sum(1 for score, label in labeled_pairs if score >= t and label == False) fn = sum(1 for score, label in labeled_pairs if score < t and label == True) precision = tp / (tp + fp) if (tp + fp) > 0 else 1.0 recall = tp / (tp + fn) if (tp + fn) > 0 else 0.0 points.append((t, precision, recall)) return points

Reading the curve

The PR curve is plotted with recall on the X axis and precision on the Y axis. Each point on the curve is one possible threshold. As you move from left to right (increasing recall), precision typically drops — you're catching more true matches, but some false ones sneak in.

The "elbow" — where to cut

Analogy — diminishing returns
Imagine you're fishing in a pond with a net. A tight-mesh net catches every fish (high recall) but also scoops up rocks and weeds (low precision). A wide-mesh net only catches the big fish (high precision) but the small ones swim through (low recall).

The elbow is the mesh size where making the net just a little tighter starts catching more rocks than fish. Past that point, you're paying a lot of precision for very little extra recall. That's your optimal threshold — the point where the trade-off turns bad.

In the table above, the elbow sits around 0.80: going from 0.85 → 0.80 gained 7 points of recall (0.78 → 0.85) while only losing 2 points of precision (0.96 → 0.94). That's a great deal. But going from 0.80 → 0.70 gained only 7 points of recall (0.85 → 0.92) while losing 7 points of precision (0.94 → 0.87). That's expensive.

The elbow isn't universal. Where it sits depends on your data. A dataset with clean, separable scores has the elbow at a high threshold. A messy dataset with overlapping distributions has it lower — or might not have a sharp elbow at all, meaning you'll always live with a painful trade-off and a bigger review queue.

Two thresholds, not one

Remember: your scorer uses two thresholds (match and review). The PR curve helps you set the match threshold — the line above which you auto-merge. But you also need the review threshold — the line below which you auto-reject.

Set the review threshold the same way, but reading the curve from the other direction: how low can you go before you start catching too many true matches in the "no match" band? The review threshold's job is to keep true matches out of the rejection zone — it's the recall floor for the review+match bands combined.

Operational reality: the review threshold also controls how big your review queue is. A wide review band (e.g. 0.40–0.80) catches more edge cases but might send 50,000 pairs to human review. A narrow band (0.70–0.85) sends fewer, but risks auto-rejecting real matches that scored in the 0.50s. Stage 06 addresses queue sizing explicitly.

Stage 05

Confidence Bands — What a Score Actually Means

A composite score of 0.75 tells you something — but what, exactly? Is it "probably a match"? "Coin flip"? "Slightly more likely than not"? The raw number is unitless. Confidence calibration maps it to a probability: "of all pairs that scored 0.75 in our labeled set, X% turned out to be true matches." That turns a number into a meaning.

The calibration table

Take your labeled data. Group pairs into score bins. For each bin, count how many were true matches vs total:

Now the score means something. A pair scoring 0.85 sits in a bin where 89.5% of historical pairs with similar scores were genuine matches. That's a statement you can reason about — and it's a lot more useful than "the number is 0.85."

Why calibration matters for thresholds

✗ Red herring — "the score is high, so it's confident"
"0.85 is a high score — clearly a match."

In Dataset A (clean CRM), 0.85 might be only moderate — true matches routinely score 0.95+. An 0.85 in that world means "something is off — maybe a field was fuzzy."

The raw score height is relative to your data distribution, not absolute.

✓ What actually decides
The match rate at that score in your data.

In Dataset B (messy vendor data), 0.85 might be the highest true matches ever score. The calibration table confirms: 89.5% match rate at that level.

Calibration tells you what a score means for this data, not in the abstract.

Confidence bands in your pipeline

Instead of just match_decision, add a confidence column. This gives downstream consumers (dashboards, analysts, the review UI) a human-readable signal:

adding a confidence column
# After computing composite and match_decision: confidence = ( F.when(F.col("composite") >= 0.90, F.lit("very_high")) .when(F.col("composite") >= 0.80, F.lit("high")) .when(F.col("composite") >= 0.70, F.lit("moderate")) .when(F.col("composite") >= 0.60, F.lit("low")) .otherwise(F.lit("very_low")) ) df = df.withColumn("confidence", confidence) # The band boundaries come from your calibration table # — the YAML config holds them alongside the thresholds.
Calibration drift: these match rates are computed from a specific labeled set at a specific time. As your source data changes (new data providers, schema changes, population shifts), the calibration can drift. Re-run the calibration quarterly or whenever you see review queue anomalies. The calibration table is a living instrument, not a one-time computation.
Hold onto: confidence bands are what the review queue operator actually sees. A reviewer looking at a pair labeled "moderate confidence — 63% match rate" makes a better decision than one looking at "composite = 0.74." The calibration table translates your scorer's internal math into a signal a human can act on.

Stage 06

Review Queue Sizing — The Operational Lever

The review band catches uncertain pairs. But "uncertain" isn't free — every pair in the review queue costs human time. If your review band is too wide, you drown the team. Too narrow, and ambiguous pairs auto-merge or auto-reject without oversight. This stage shows you how to size the queue so it's operationally viable.

The three-way budget

Every pair goes to one of three places. The thresholds control how many go where:

DestinationThreshold controlCost
Auto-mergescore ≥ match_thresholdZero human cost — but false merges corrupt records.
Review queuereview_threshold ≤ score < match_thresholdHuman time per pair — typically 1–3 minutes each.
Auto-rejectscore < review_thresholdZero human cost — but false rejections leave duplicates unresolved.

Worked example — sizing the queue

Your pipeline produces 50,000 candidate pairs per run. Here's how different threshold settings distribute them:

match=0.90, review=0.50		8,000 (16%)18,000 (36%)24,000 (48%)~600 hrs ❌

match=0.80, review=0.60		14,000 (28%)9,000 (18%)27,000 (54%)~300 hrs ✓

The conservative setting sends 36% of pairs to review — 600 hours of human work. If your team has 2 reviewers doing 8 hours/day, that's 37 business days to clear the queue. The aggressive setting clears it in 6 days — but it's auto-merging pairs with only 70% composite scores, which may include false merges.

The capacity formula

queue sizing calculation
review_pairs = pairs_in_review_band
= 9,000 pairs (from the balanced setting)
review_time_per_pair = 2 minutes (your team's average)
total_hours = 9,000 × 2 / 60 = 300 hours
team_capacity = reviewers × hours_per_day × days_until_next_run
= 2 × 8 × 20 = 320 hours/month
300 < 320 → the queue just fits within one pipeline cycle. Safe to ship.
Queue overflow is the silent killer of ER pipelines. If the review queue is bigger than the team can process before the next pipeline run, it grows monotonically — every run adds more pairs than reviewers can clear. Within a few cycles, the queue is months behind, reviews are stale (the underlying records may have changed), and the whole review process collapses. Size the queue to fit your team, not the other way around.

The tuning loop, end to end

Here's the full decision process for setting both thresholds:

Step 1
Set match_threshold
Use the PR curve. Find the elbow or the precision you need. This determines how many pairs auto-merge and at what quality.
Step 2
Set review_threshold
Pick the point where the review queue fits your team's capacity. Lower = safer (catches more edge cases) but more expensive. Raise until the queue fits.
Step 3
Validate on labeled data
Check: how many true matches land in the rejection zone (below review_threshold)? If more than you can tolerate, widen the review band — or improve the scorer itself.
This is iterative, not one-shot. You tune, run a pipeline, sample new pairs for labeling, recalibrate, and tune again. Each cycle tightens the thresholds closer to optimal. The first pass gets you from "guessing" to "roughly right." The second pass gets you to "production-ready."

confusion_matrix — counting TP/FP/FN/TN at one threshold

This method's whole job: given a cutoff score, sort every pair into one of four buckets — caught a real match (TP), wrongly merged (FP), wrongly rejected (FN), or correctly rejected (TN).

ThresholdTuner.confusion_matrix (PySpark)
def confusion_matrix(self, match_threshold): is_match = F.col(self.label_col) == "true_match" predicted = F.col(self.score_col) >= match_threshold return self.df.select( F.sum(F.when( predicted & is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("tp"), F.sum(F.when( predicted & ~is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("fp"), F.sum(F.when(~predicted & is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("fn"), F.sum(F.when(~predicted & ~is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("tn"), ).first()
What F.when(cond, 1).otherwise(0) does: read it as "for every row: 1 if cond is true, else 0" — it's PySpark's per-row if/else. F.sum(...) then adds that 0/1 column across all rows into a single number. This is the classification-pattern idiom: build a per-row condition, turn it into a 0/1, then sum.
What & and ~ mean here: predicted and is_match are each a column of True/False — one value per row, not a single boolean. & and ~ are PySpark's per-row AND / NOT for columns; Python's own and/not can't be used here because there's no single value to test.
One caveat before you predict: the trace below shows row P1, then P2, then P3... as if Spark works through them one at a time. That's the logical result, not literally how it runs — Spark evaluates this across the whole column at once, in parallel, possibly out of row order. The table is the right mental model for "what answer comes out," not for "what order things happen in."

pr_at — precision/recall from those counts

This method's job: turn the four raw counts into the two numbers that actually answer "is this threshold any good" — precision (of what we merged, how much was right) and recall (of what should've merged, how much did we catch).

ThresholdTuner.pr_at (PySpark + plain Python)
def pr_at(self, match_threshold): cm = self.confusion_matrix(match_threshold) tp, fp, fn = cm.tp, cm.fp, cm.fn precision = tp / (tp + fp) if (tp + fp) > 0 else 1.0 recall = tp / (tp + fn) if (tp + fn) > 0 else 0.0 return {"threshold": match_threshold, "precision": precision, "recall": recall, "tp": tp, "fp": fp, "fn": fn}
What cm.tp is doing: .first() (inside confusion_matrix) returns one Row object — think of it like a dict you can also dot-access. cm.tp reaches into that Row and pulls out the column named "tp" by name. Everything after that line is plain Python arithmetic — nothing PySpark-specific.

calibration_table — match rate per score bin

This method's job: bucket pairs by score range and check whether the score is actually trustworthy — does "0.8-something" really mean an 80% chance of being a real match?

ThresholdTuner.calibration_table (PySpark)
def calibration_table(self, bins=10): binned = self.df.withColumn( "bin", (F.col(self.score_col) * bins).cast("int") / bins ) return binned.groupBy("bin").agg( F.count("*").alias("total"), F.sum(F.when( F.col(self.label_col) == "true_match", 1 ).otherwise(0)).alias("matches"), ).withColumn( "match_rate", F.col("matches") / F.col("total") ).orderBy("bin")
.withColumn("bin", expr): adds a column to every row — it doesn't change self.df in place, it returns a brand-new DataFrame. You've seen this idiom once before (Module 01); here it's just computing which bin a score falls into.
.groupBy("bin").agg(...) — the pivot-table move: this is the SQL GROUP BY equivalent. Before this call, you have 8 rows (one per pair). groupBy("bin") says "collapse every row that shares the same bin value into one row." .agg(F.count("*"), F.sum(...)) says what to compute per group. Concretely: P2 (bin 0.8) and P3 (bin 0.8) go in as two separate rows and come out as one row — bin=0.8, total=2, matches=1 — while every other bin in this toy set has only one row in it, so it passes through as total=1.
.orderBy("bin"): sorts the grouped result, ascending by default. This isn't cosmetic — groupBy on its own makes no promise about what order the output rows come out in (Spark groups across a distributed cluster; there's no inherent "first bin, second bin" order until you ask for one). Without .orderBy, the calibration table could print bins in any order on different runs.
Predict before you reveal: the bin expression is (F.col(score_col) * 10).cast("int"). For P2, composite = 0.88, so 0.88 * 10 = 8.8. What integer does .cast("int") produce for 8.8 — does it round to the nearest integer, or truncate?

queue_size — counting pairs in each operational band

This method's job is domain logic you've already seen the syntax for — same boolean-mask-and-sum pattern as confusion_matrix, just bucketing by score range instead of by TP/FP/FN/TN.

def queue_size(self, full_df, match_t, review_t): return full_df.select( F.sum(F.when(F.col("composite") >= match_t, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("auto_merge"), F.sum(F.when( (F.col("composite") >= review_t) & (F.col("composite") < match_t), 1 ).otherwise(0)).alias("review"), F.sum(F.when(F.col("composite") < review_t, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("auto_reject"), ).first()

ThresholdTuner — the assembled pattern (PySpark)
from pyspark.sql import functions as F from pyspark.sql import Window class ThresholdTuner: """Evaluates and tunes match/review thresholds against labeled data.""" def __init__(self, labeled_df, score_col="composite", label_col="human_label"): self.df = labeled_df self.score_col = score_col self.label_col = label_col def confusion_matrix(self, match_threshold): is_match = F.col(self.label_col) == "true_match" predicted = F.col(self.score_col) >= match_threshold return self.df.select( F.sum(F.when( predicted & is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("tp"), F.sum(F.when( predicted & ~is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("fp"), F.sum(F.when(~predicted & is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("fn"), F.sum(F.when(~predicted & ~is_match, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("tn"), ).first() def pr_at(self, match_threshold): cm = self.confusion_matrix(match_threshold) tp, fp, fn = cm.tp, cm.fp, cm.fn precision = tp / (tp + fp) if (tp + fp) > 0 else 1.0 recall = tp / (tp + fn) if (tp + fn) > 0 else 0.0 return {"threshold": match_threshold, "precision": precision, "recall": recall, "tp": tp, "fp": fp, "fn": fn} def pr_curve(self, steps=20): thresholds = [i / steps for i in range(steps + 1)] return [self.pr_at(t) for t in thresholds] def calibration_table(self, bins=10): binned = self.df.withColumn( "bin", (F.col(self.score_col) * bins).cast("int") / bins ) return binned.groupBy("bin").agg( F.count("*").alias("total"), F.sum(F.when( F.col(self.label_col) == "true_match", 1 ).otherwise(0)).alias("matches"), ).withColumn( "match_rate", F.col("matches") / F.col("total") ).orderBy("bin") def queue_size(self, full_df, match_t, review_t): return full_df.select( F.sum(F.when(F.col("composite") >= match_t, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("auto_merge"), F.sum(F.when( (F.col("composite") >= review_t) & (F.col("composite") < match_t), 1 ).otherwise(0)).alias("review"), F.sum(F.when(F.col("composite") < review_t, 1).otherwise(0)).alias("auto_reject"), ).first()

Honest Gaps
What this module didn't cover
F1 score: the harmonic mean of precision and recall. Mentioned in the widget but not drilled — it's a useful single-number summary but doesn't replace understanding the P/R trade-off directly.

Automated threshold selection: algorithms like Youden's J statistic or the F-beta optimizer that pick the "best" threshold mathematically. These exist and are useful but require the foundation this module just built.

Active learning: instead of labeling random pairs, selecting the pairs near the decision boundary for labeling to get maximum information per label. Important for efficiency when labeling is expensive — a future session.

Multi-threshold optimization: tuning match_threshold and review_threshold jointly (rather than sequentially) using a grid search over both. The widget approximates this but the formal approach needs a labeled set larger than this module assumed.
